# Chapter 3: The Free Tools You Should Be Using Today

You don't need to spend money to start using AI in your business. I want to be clear about that upfront, because there's a whole industry of people who would love to sell you a $49/month subscription before you even know what you're subscribing to.

The tools in this chapter are free. Actually free — not "free for 7 days and then we charge your card" free. Each one has a free tier that is genuinely useful for a small business. Some of them have paid upgrades that might be worth it down the road, and I'll tell you what those cost too. But you can do real work with the free versions, starting today.

I'm going to walk through six tools. For each one, I'll tell you what it costs, what it's good for, what will trip you up, and one thing you can try right now. Not tomorrow. Not after you "do more research." Right now, today, in the time it takes to drink a cup of coffee.

Let's go.

---

## ChatGPT: Your New First Draft Machine

**What it is:** ChatGPT is the one you've heard of. It's made by a company called OpenAI, and it's the tool that kicked off this whole AI moment. You talk to it like you're texting someone, and it writes back.

**What it costs:** Free. You go to chat.openai.com, make an account with your email, and start typing. That's it. The free tier uses a model that's slightly older than the cutting-edge version, but for most small business tasks, you will not notice the difference. The paid version (ChatGPT Plus) is $20/month and gives you faster responses, access to the newest model, and some image generation features. You don't need it yet.

**What it's actually good for:**

Drafting. Brainstorming. Rewriting things you've already written.

Let me be specific. Say you need to write a description for a new service you're offering. You've been staring at a blank screen for twenty minutes. That's where ChatGPT shines. You give it the rough idea, and it gives you a first draft. The first draft won't be perfect — it'll sound a little generic, a little too polished, maybe a little too enthusiastic. But it's not a blank screen anymore. You can edit a draft. You can't edit nothing.

It's also good for brainstorming. Need ten ideas for a summer promotion? Ask it. Need five different ways to word a policy for your employee handbook? Ask it. You'll throw out half of what it gives you, and that's fine. The point is to get unstuck.

And rewriting — this is maybe the most underrated use. You've written an email to a difficult customer and you know it sounds too angry. Paste it in and say "rewrite this to be professional but firm." That alone is worth the five minutes it takes to sign up.

**What it's NOT good for:**

Facts. Math. Anything where being wrong matters.

I cannot stress this enough. ChatGPT will confidently tell you things that are completely made up. It will invent statistics. It will cite studies that don't exist. It will give you a phone number for a business that closed three years ago. It does this with the same confident tone it uses when it's right, so you can't tell the difference by how it sounds.

Do not use it to look up tax rules. Do not use it to check legal requirements. Do not use it to verify anything. Use it to *write*, not to *know*.

**Example prompt for a Lancaster business:**

Let's say you run a landscaping company and you need a Google Business Profile description.

> "I own a landscaping company in Lancaster, Ohio called Green Valley Landscaping. We do residential lawn care, mulching, spring and fall cleanups, and small hardscape projects like patios and retaining walls. We've been in business for 12 years and serve Fairfield County. Write me a Google Business Profile description that's friendly but professional. Keep it under 200 words."

You'll get something usable in about four seconds. Read it, change anything that doesn't sound like you, and post it.

**The gotcha:**

ChatGPT remembers your conversation as you go, but it does NOT remember previous conversations unless you turn on a memory feature (and even then, it's inconsistent). So if you spent twenty minutes giving it context about your business last Tuesday, it doesn't know any of that today. You'll need to give it context again each time, or keep one long conversation going for related tasks.

Also: anything you type into ChatGPT can potentially be used to train future versions of the model. Don't paste in sensitive customer data, financial information, or anything you wouldn't want a stranger to read. There's a setting to turn off training data usage — if you're going to use it regularly, go to Settings > Data Controls and toggle that off.

**Try this right now:**

Go to chat.openai.com. Sign up. Then type this:

> "I own a [your type of business] in Lancaster, Ohio. Write me three different social media posts I could use this week. Make them casual and friendly, not salesy."

Read what comes back. Edit it into something that sounds like you. Post one of them. You just saved yourself thirty minutes of staring at your phone trying to think of something to post.

## Claude: The Careful One

**What it is:** Claude is made by a company called Anthropic. It's the same basic idea as ChatGPT — you type, it writes back — but it has a different personality. If ChatGPT is the enthusiastic new employee who's eager to help, Claude is the thoughtful one who reads the whole email before responding.

**What it costs:** Free at claude.ai. Sign up with your email or Google account. The free tier gives you a generous amount of usage per day. The paid version (Claude Pro) is $20/month and gives you significantly more usage and access to the most powerful model. Again, start free.

**What it's good for:**

Longer, more complex tasks. If you need to take a five-page lease agreement and summarize the key points, Claude tends to be more thorough and careful about it than ChatGPT. If you need to give it complicated instructions — "rewrite this but keep the tone formal in the first paragraph, casual in the second, and include a call to action at the end that mentions our Saturday hours" — Claude tends to follow multi-step instructions more reliably.

Claude is also good when you want a more measured, less hype-y tone. ChatGPT sometimes writes like a marketing brochure even when you don't want it to. Claude tends to be a bit more restrained. For some tasks, that's exactly what you need.

I'd also reach for Claude when I want to think through a business decision. Not because it will make the decision for you — it won't, and it shouldn't — but because it's genuinely good at helping you think through pros and cons, surface things you haven't considered, and organize your own thinking.

**Example prompt for a Lancaster business:**

Say you're thinking about extending your store hours on Thursdays to capture the evening crowd.

> "I own a retail shop in downtown Lancaster, Ohio. I'm thinking about extending my hours from 6pm to 8pm on Thursdays. Currently my busiest hours are 11am-2pm on weekdays and Saturday mornings. I have two part-time employees. Help me think through the pros and cons of this decision. What should I consider that I might not have thought of? Be honest — don't just tell me it's a great idea."

Claude will give you a genuinely useful framework for thinking it through. It might mention staffing costs, the Farmer's Market traffic pattern on certain evenings, the importance of testing it for a set period before committing, and the need to actually market the extended hours so people know about them.

**The gotcha:**

Claude's free tier has daily usage limits. If you're in the middle of a big project — say, rewriting your entire employee handbook — you might hit the limit partway through. This is annoying but not a dealbreaker. You can pick it up the next day, or you can switch to ChatGPT to finish. Having accounts on both is free, so there's no reason not to.

Also, like ChatGPT, Claude can be wrong about facts. It's generally a bit more cautious — it's more likely to say "I'm not sure about this" rather than confidently making something up — but it's still an AI. Verify anything that matters.

**Try this right now:**

Go to claude.ai. Sign up. Then paste in something you've already written — an About page, a customer email, a job posting — and type:

> "Here's something I wrote for my business. Tell me honestly: what's unclear, what's too long, and what could be better? Don't be nice about it — be useful."

The feedback you get will be surprisingly specific and genuinely helpful.

## Google Gemini: The One That's Already in Your Stuff

**What it is:** Gemini is Google's AI. If you have a Google account — and if you use Gmail or Google Docs for your business, you do — then you already have access to it. You can use it at gemini.google.com, or increasingly, it shows up right inside Google Docs, Sheets, and Gmail as a little sparkle icon or a side panel.

**What it costs:** Free with your Google account. The paid version (Google One AI Premium) is $19.99/month and bakes Gemini more deeply into your Google Workspace apps. The free version is where you should start.

**What it's good for:**

Working with things that are already in your Google ecosystem. If your business runs on Google Docs and Google Sheets — and a lot of small businesses do, because they're free — then Gemini is the most convenient AI to reach for because it's already there.

Need to write a reply to an email in Gmail? Gemini can draft one. Need to summarize a long Google Doc? Gemini can do that without you having to copy and paste anything. Need to create a basic spreadsheet? You can describe what you want in plain English.

Gemini is also decent as a general-purpose AI for the same kinds of tasks ChatGPT and Claude handle — drafting, brainstorming, rewriting. It's not as strong as either of those for pure writing quality, in my experience, but it's good enough for a lot of tasks, and the convenience of having it built into tools you already use matters.

**Example prompt for a Lancaster business:**

Say you've got a Google Sheet with your inventory and you need to make sense of it.

Open Gemini (gemini.google.com) and type:

> "I have a Google Sheet with three columns: Product Name, Quantity in Stock, and Last Order Date. I want to add a column that flags any product where the quantity is below 10 and the last order was more than 30 days ago. Write me the formula for that, and explain it like I'm not a spreadsheet person."

Gemini will give you the formula AND explain what each part does. That's legitimately useful. We'll talk more about the spreadsheet angle in a minute.

**The gotcha:**

Gemini is newer to this game than ChatGPT and Claude, and it shows sometimes. It can be more inconsistent — great on one task, mediocre on the next. It also has a tendency to be aggressively helpful in ways that aren't always helpful, like giving you a much longer answer than you needed.

The bigger issue is privacy. If you use Gemini inside your Google Workspace, it has access to your emails, your documents, your calendar. That's the whole point — that's what makes it convenient. But it means you should understand that Google's AI is reading your stuff. For most small businesses, this isn't a dealbreaker (Google already processes your data for search and spam filtering), but it's worth knowing.

**Try this right now:**

Go to gemini.google.com while logged into your Google account. Type:

> "Write me a short, professional out-of-office reply for email. I'll be out from [date] to [date]. Keep it simple — I don't need anything cute."

Use it next time you're actually out. Done. You're now a person who uses AI.

## Canva AI: Social Media Graphics Without the Headache

**What it is:** Canva is a design tool that's been around for years. You've maybe used it to make a flyer or a Facebook post. Recently they've added AI features that make it significantly more useful, especially for people who are not designers and don't want to become one.

**What it costs:** Free tier is genuinely useful. You get access to thousands of templates, basic AI image generation, and AI text suggestions. Canva Pro is $12.99/month (or $119.99/year) and gets you more templates, more AI features, brand kit tools, and the ability to resize designs for different platforms with one click. The free tier is enough to start.

**What it's good for:**

Making your social media posts look like a professional made them, even though you made them in eleven minutes between customers.

The AI features let you do things like: describe an image you want and have it generated, remove backgrounds from photos with one click, resize a design from Instagram square to Facebook banner automatically, and generate text for your designs.

But honestly, the biggest value is the templates. You search for "small business sale Instagram post" and you get hundreds of professionally designed templates. You swap in your text, your colors, maybe your logo, and you have something that looks real. Not homemade. Not clip-art-on-a-Word-document.

**Example use case for a Lancaster business:**

Say you own a bakery and you want to promote a weekend special.

Open Canva. Search templates for "bakery social media." Pick one you like. Change the text to say "This Saturday Only: Buckeye Brownies, $2 each, Lancaster location." If you have a decent phone photo of the brownies, drop it in. If not, use Canva's AI image generator — type "close-up photo of chocolate brownies with peanut butter filling on parchment paper" and it'll create something that looks reasonable.

Export. Post. That took you less time than it would take to describe what you want to a graphic designer, let alone wait for them to send a proof.

**The gotcha:**

The AI-generated images are getting better, but they can still look a little... off. Weird textures. Odd lighting. Hands with too many fingers if there are people in the image. Always look at the image carefully before you post it. And if you're selling a specific product, a real photo from your phone will always be more authentic than an AI-generated one. Use AI generation for backgrounds, decorative elements, or when you need a generic mood-setting image. Use your own photos for your actual products.

Also, the free tier puts Canva watermarks on some premium elements. If you design something and a little crown icon appears in the corner of a graphic element, that means it's a paid element. You'll either need to swap it for a free one or upgrade. This is annoying but manageable.

**Try this right now:**

Go to canva.com. Sign up (free). Click "Create a design" and choose "Instagram Post." In the templates panel, search for something relevant to your business. Pick a template, change the text to promote something you actually offer, and download it. Post it today. You just made a professional-looking graphic in less time than it takes to eat lunch.

## Voice-to-Text: Turn Your Rambling Into Content

**What it is:** Voice-to-text tools record what you say and turn it into written text. The one I recommend is Monologue (monologue.to). It's a voice dictation app for Mac and iPhone that adapts to your writing style, your vocabulary, and your context. It doesn't just transcribe — it understands what you meant and cleans it up as you go.

**What it costs:** Monologue is included free with a subscription to Every (every.to), which is $20/month. I'm going to recommend Every anyway — more on that in a moment — so if you subscribe, Monologue comes with it at no extra cost.

Now, $20/month for a newsletter might sound steep. But Every isn't a typical newsletter. It's a publication that covers what real businesses are actually doing with AI — not hype pieces, not press releases, but practical reporting on what's working and what isn't. For $20/month, you get Monologue, you get a constant stream of honest AI business reporting, and you get access to other tools they build. It's the one subscription I'd recommend to any small business owner who wants to stay informed without drowning in noise.

If you don't want to pay for anything, you can use the Voice Memos app on your iPhone or the Recorder app on Android (which has built-in transcription on Pixel phones) to record yourself, then paste the transcription into ChatGPT or Claude for cleanup. This costs nothing.

**What it's good for:**

Here's the thing about most small business owners I've met: they can *talk* about their business better than they can *write* about it. Put a keyboard in front of them and they freeze up. Put them in a conversation and they'll explain their business beautifully for ten minutes straight.

Voice-to-text tools exploit this. Instead of trying to write a blog post, you talk about the topic for five minutes. You ramble, you go on tangents, you repeat yourself — that's fine. Then you take that transcription and paste it into ChatGPT or Claude with a prompt like:

> "Here's a transcription of me talking about [topic]. Turn this into a blog post of about 500 words. Keep my voice and my personality — don't make it sound corporate. Clean up the rambling but keep the good stuff."

What comes back will sound like you, because it started as you. The AI just organized it and cleaned up the "ums."

This also works for meeting notes. Have Monologue running during a meeting with a contractor or a planning session with your employees. Afterward, you have a searchable transcript. Ask the AI to pull out action items and decisions. That beats scribbled notes on a legal pad that you'll lose by Thursday.

**Example use case for a Lancaster business:**

You own a plumbing company and you want to post helpful tips on your website (this is great for local SEO, by the way, but we'll get to that later). You know everything about preventing frozen pipes in an Ohio winter, but writing about it sounds like homework.

Instead: open Monologue on your phone (or Voice Memos if you're using the free route). Hit record. Talk for three to five minutes about what homeowners should do before the first hard freeze. Stop recording. Monologue will give you a clean transcription that already understands your technical vocabulary — it learns words like "PEX" and "shutoff valve" and "heat tape" after hearing you use them once. Then paste it into ChatGPT or Claude:

> "Here's a transcription of me talking about preventing frozen pipes. Turn it into a helpful blog post for homeowners in central Ohio. Keep it practical and friendly. I want to sound like a knowledgeable neighbor, not a textbook."

You now have a blog post. It took you less than fifteen minutes total. A content writer would've charged you $150 for the same thing, and it wouldn't have had your actual expertise in it.

**The gotcha:**

Transcription quality depends heavily on audio quality. If you're recording in a noisy shop or with your phone in your pocket, the transcription will be a mess. Find a quiet-ish spot, hold your phone at a normal distance, and speak at a normal pace. You don't need a recording studio, but you do need to not be standing next to a table saw.

Also, most voice-to-text tools send your audio to the cloud for processing. Monologue is built on open models, which is a plus for transparency, but your audio still goes somewhere. If you're discussing sensitive business information, customer details, or anything confidential, be aware of that. Read the privacy policy. Or just be thoughtful about what you record.

**Try this right now:**

Open Monologue on your phone (or Voice Memos if you're using the free route). Record yourself answering this question out loud: "What's the one thing my customers always ask me about, and what's the answer?" Talk for two to three minutes. Then grab the transcription and paste it into ChatGPT or Claude with this prompt:

> "Turn this into a short FAQ answer I could put on my website. Keep it under 200 words and make it sound like a real person, not a corporation."

You now have website content. It took you five minutes.

## Google Sheets + AI: Your Spreadsheet Just Got Smarter

**What it is:** This isn't a separate tool — it's using the AI tools you already have (ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini) to help you work with spreadsheets. If the thought of writing a spreadsheet formula makes you break out in a cold sweat, this section is for you.

**What it costs:** Free. You're combining Google Sheets (free) with AI chatbots (free tiers).

**What it's good for:**

Three things: writing formulas, cleaning up messy data, and making sense of numbers.

**Writing formulas:** Instead of Googling "how to write a VLOOKUP" and falling down a forty-minute rabbit hole, you describe what you want in plain English to an AI:

> "I have a Google Sheet. Column A is customer names, Column B is their email addresses, Column C is the date of their last purchase. Write me a formula for Column D that says 'Follow Up' if their last purchase was more than 90 days ago, and 'Active' if it was within 90 days."

The AI will give you the exact formula. You paste it into your sheet. It works. You just did in two minutes what would've taken you half an hour of frustrated Googling.

**Cleaning up data:** Say you imported a customer list from somewhere and the phone numbers are in five different formats — some have dashes, some have parentheses, some have spaces, some have nothing. You can describe the problem to an AI and get a formula or a set of steps to standardize everything.

**Making sense of numbers:** You can paste a small data set into an AI and ask plain-English questions. "Here are my monthly sales numbers for the last year. Which months were my strongest? Is there a seasonal pattern? What should I expect for next quarter based on this?" The AI won't have magical insight, but it can spot patterns and do basic analysis faster than you can in your head.

**Example use case for a Lancaster business:**

You run a small retail store and you track inventory in a Google Sheet. You want to know which items haven't sold in 60 days so you can put them on clearance.

Open ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini and type:

> "In my Google Sheet, Column A has product names, Column B has quantities, and Column C has the date of the last sale. Write me a formula that highlights the row in red if the last sale was more than 60 days ago and the quantity is greater than 5. Give me step-by-step instructions for how to set this up, including conditional formatting. Assume I know how to use a spreadsheet but I don't know advanced features."

Follow the steps. You now have a sheet that automatically flags dead inventory. Your bookkeeper would be proud.

**The gotcha:**

If your spreadsheet has sensitive data — customer names, email addresses, financial figures — do NOT paste the actual data into ChatGPT or Claude. Instead, describe the structure of your sheet (Column A is names, Column B is emails, etc.) or use fake sample data. The AI doesn't need your real data to write you a formula. It just needs to know the structure.

Also, always test the formula on a small sample before applying it to your whole sheet. AI-generated formulas are usually right, but "usually right" and "always right" are very different things when you're talking about your business data.

**Try this right now:**

Think of one spreadsheet you use regularly where you do something manually that feels repetitive. Open ChatGPT or Claude and describe what you do:

> "Every week I manually go through my spreadsheet and [describe the task]. Is there a formula or a feature in Google Sheets that could do this automatically? Explain it to me like I'm a competent adult who just doesn't know spreadsheet tricks."

You'll probably get a solution. And you'll probably be annoyed that you've been doing it manually for two years.

## A Few Ground Rules for All of These

Before we move on, a handful of things that apply to every tool in this chapter:

**Don't share sensitive data.** I've said it for individual tools, but it bears repeating as a general rule. Customer social security numbers, credit card info, medical information, employee personnel details — none of this goes into any AI tool. Ever. Use fake data or describe the structure instead.

**Verify anything that matters.** If the AI tells you a tax deadline, a legal requirement, a regulation, or a statistic — verify it from an actual authoritative source before acting on it. AI tools are writing tools, not research tools.

**You are the editor, not the audience.** Everything these tools produce is a first draft. Read it. Change it. Make it sound like you. If you post AI-generated text without reading it, you will eventually post something embarrassing. I've seen it happen. It's not pretty.

**It's okay to use more than one.** ChatGPT for quick brainstorming, Claude for longer documents, Gemini for spreadsheet help, Canva for graphics. They're free. There's no rule that says you pick one and stick with it. Use whatever works best for the task in front of you.

**Start with one thing.** I just threw six tools at you. Don't try to use all of them this week. Pick one — whichever one solves the most annoying problem you have right now — and use it for a week. Get comfortable. Then add another. This is a slow game, and the people who win it are the ones who actually use the tools, not the ones who sign up for everything and use nothing.
