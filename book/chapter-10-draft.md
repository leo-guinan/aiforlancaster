# Chapter 10: Custom Automations — When Off-the-Shelf Isn't Enough

If you've been following along through this book, you've already got a pretty solid toolkit. ChatGPT for writing and thinking. A voice agent answering your phones. Maybe some social media help. For a lot of businesses in Lancaster, that's the whole ballgame. You can stop here, save some money, win back some hours, and go fishing.

But some of you are still reading because you've got a nagging feeling. You've got workflows that are held together with sticky notes and memory. You've got three different software tools that don't talk to each other. You've got a process that works, technically, but it requires someone to manually copy information from one place to another six times a day, and every time they forget, something falls through the cracks.

This chapter is for you. It's about what happens when the off-the-shelf stuff isn't enough, and you need something built for the way your business actually works.

Fair warning: this is where costs go up. Not dramatically — not like buying a new truck or leasing a storefront — but we're no longer in the land of free tiers and $20/month subscriptions. So I'm going to be direct about when this makes sense and when it doesn't.


## CRM: A System That Remembers Your Customers So You Don't Have To

Let's start with CRM, because it's the thing most businesses need and the thing most business owners' eyes glaze over about.

CRM stands for Customer Relationship Management. That sounds like something a Fortune 500 company invented to justify a department. Here's what it actually means: it's a system that keeps track of every customer you've ever talked to, what you talked about, what they bought, when they bought it, and when you should follow up.

You know how your best salesperson — the one who's been with you fifteen years — just *knows* that Mrs. Henderson prefers to be called in the morning, that she always needs her furnace checked in October, and that her daughter just had a baby? A CRM is the software version of that person's brain. Except it doesn't retire, it doesn't call in sick, and it doesn't take all that knowledge with it when it leaves for a competitor.

If you've got fewer than fifty customers and a good memory, you might not need one. If you've got more than that, or if you've ever lost a sale because nobody followed up, you need one.

### The Free Options (Start Here)

Before you spend a dime, try the free stuff.

**HubSpot Free Tier.** HubSpot is a big company that makes CRM software. Their free tier is legitimately useful — not a "free trial" that expires, but an actual free product. You can store up to a million contacts, track deals, log emails, and set reminders to follow up. The catch? They want you to eventually upgrade to their paid plans, which start around $20/month and go way up from there. But plenty of small businesses run on the free tier for years.

**Google Contacts + Google Sheets.** This is the duct-tape version. You keep your contacts in Google Contacts, and you track your deals and follow-ups in a Google Sheet. It's not elegant. It doesn't automate anything. But it's free, it works, and if you've got a business with under a hundred active customers, it might be all you need. I know a landscaping company in Fairfield County that's run this way for three years and it works fine.

### When You Need Something Custom

Here's where things get interesting.

The free CRMs are built for everyone, which means they're built for no one in particular. If you're a plumber, your workflow is different from a real estate agent, which is different from a dog grooming salon. The generic CRM doesn't know that. It gives you the same fields, the same layout, the same process — and you end up bending your workflow to match the software instead of the other way around.

This is where the custom CRM comes in. And thanks to what's happening in software development right now, these are dramatically cheaper than they were even two years ago.

There's a revolution happening called "vibe coding" — which is a silly name for a real thing. Tools like Replit, Cursor, and similar platforms let developers (and increasingly, non-developers) build custom applications using AI assistance. You describe what you want, the AI writes most of the code, and a developer cleans it up and makes it work. What used to take a team of five developers three months can now be done by one person in a few weeks.

Here's a real example I heard on the My First Million podcast: a guy started building custom CRM applications for small businesses. Each one cost the client about $15,000. He did it with a small team using AI-assisted development. His first year, he did $2.5 million in revenue with 60% margins. That's not because he was ripping people off — it's because the tools have gotten that much faster and cheaper to build with. His clients were getting custom software that would have cost $50,000-$100,000 three years ago, and they were getting it for $15,000.

Now, $15,000 is real money. I'm not going to pretend it's pocket change. But think about it this way.

### The HVAC Example

Say you're an HVAC company. You've got 30 crews. You're paying $4,000 a month for ServiceTitan, which is the big enterprise software in that industry. That's $48,000 a year. And you're probably only using 40% of its features, because ServiceTitan is built for companies with 200 crews, not 30.

A custom tool — built for your specific dispatch process, your specific service area, your specific pricing — might cost $15,000 to build and $200/month to maintain. In four months, you've broken even compared to ServiceTitan. After that, you're saving $3,800 a month. That's $45,600 a year, every year.

That's when custom makes sense. Not because it's cool. Not because it's cutting-edge. Because the math works.


## Connecting Your Existing Tools: Zapier and Make

Maybe you don't need a full custom application. Maybe you just need your existing tools to talk to each other.

This is what Zapier and Make do. (Make used to be called Integromat, if you've heard that name.) The concept is simple:

**When X happens, automatically do Y.**

That's it. That's the whole idea. Here are some real examples:

- When someone fills out the contact form on your website → automatically create a contact in your CRM → send them a welcome email → assign them to a salesperson → create a task to follow up in 24 hours.

- When someone leaves a Google review → send you a Slack message (or a text) → if it's negative, create a high-priority task to respond immediately.

- When a new invoice is marked "paid" in QuickBooks → update the customer record in your CRM → send a thank-you email → add them to your "repeat customer" email list.

- When someone books an appointment through your website → add it to your Google Calendar → send a confirmation email → send a reminder text 24 hours before.

Each of these would take a human 2-5 minutes to do manually. If you've got 20 new inquiries a day, that's an hour or two of someone's time just doing data entry and sending emails. Every day. Zapier or Make does it in seconds, automatically, without forgetting or making typos.

### The Free Tiers

Both Zapier and Make have free tiers. Zapier's free plan gives you 100 tasks per month with simple two-step automations. Make's free plan gives you 1,000 operations per month. For a small business just getting started with automation, these free tiers might be enough to test the concept and see if it's worth investing more.

Paid plans start around $20/month for Zapier and $9/month for Make. Not exactly budget-busting.

### The Honest Take

Here's where I level with you: these tools are powerful, but they're not as simple as the marketing makes them sound.

The concept is easy — when X happens, do Y. Actually setting it up requires you to understand how your tools connect, what data they share, and how to handle the edge cases. What happens when someone fills out the form but leaves the phone number blank? What happens when the email bounces? What happens when the same person fills out the form twice?

Budget 5-10 hours to learn one of these platforms. Not 30 minutes. Not "just a quick setup." Five to ten hours of watching tutorials, building test automations, breaking things, and fixing them. If that sounds like a lot, remember: you're building a system that will save you hours every week for years. The ROI is there. But the upfront learning is real, and anyone who tells you it takes 15 minutes is selling you something.

If you don't have 5-10 hours and the idea of learning a new software platform makes you want to close this book, that's fine. This is one of the things a consultant can set up for you. (More on that in Chapter 11.)


## Custom Apps for Your Specific Workflow

This is the high end. The deep end of the pool. If CRM and automation connectors are the standard tools, custom apps are the specialty equipment.

A custom app is a piece of software built specifically for your business. Not adapted, not configured, not "customized" — built. From scratch. For you.

### What This Costs

$5,000 to $15,000 depending on complexity. Possibly more if your needs are genuinely complex, but for most small businesses in Lancaster, that range covers it.

That might sound like a lot. It might sound like a little. It depends on what you're comparing it to.

### When It Makes Sense

You're paying for three or four different software subscriptions that don't talk to each other. Your office manager spends two hours a day copying data between systems. You've got a workflow that's specific to your industry — or specific to the way *you* do business — and no off-the-shelf tool handles it well.

Here's a concrete example. Say you run a property management company. You've got:

- A tenant portal (for rent payments and maintenance requests): $150/month
- A separate accounting system: $80/month  
- A separate maintenance dispatch tool: $120/month
- Google Sheets for tracking vacancies and showings
- A paper system for lease renewals that someone has to manually check every month

That's $350/month in software that doesn't connect, plus hours of manual work bridging the gaps. A custom app that handles all of it — tenant portal, accounting integration, maintenance dispatch, vacancy tracking, lease management — might cost $12,000 to build and $200/month to run.

Payback period: about a year when you factor in the software savings and the labor savings. After that, it's pure upside.

### When It Doesn't Make Sense

You're a three-person business and Excel still works fine. Seriously. If your current system isn't broken — if you're not losing customers, dropping tasks, or wasting hours on manual work — don't fix it. A custom app is a solution to a real problem, not a status symbol.

I've talked to business owners who want a custom app because they think it'll make them look more professional. It won't. Your customers don't care what software you use. They care whether you show up on time, do good work, and answer when they call.

If your business has fewer than ten employees and your current tools work, the answer is almost certainly: don't build a custom app. Use the free CRM. Set up a couple of Zapier automations. Save your money for something that actually moves the needle.


## The Maintenance Reality

I need to end this chapter with the thing nobody wants to hear.

Nothing is "set and forget."

Not your CRM. Not your automations. Not your custom app. Nothing.

Software needs updates. The tools you connect to change their systems. Google updates their API. QuickBooks changes a field name. Your business evolves — you add a service line, you hire more people, you change your pricing structure. All of that means your automations and custom tools need to evolve too.

Budget for this. Seriously. Write it down.

For Zapier/Make automations: plan to spend 1-2 hours per month checking that everything's still running and fixing anything that broke. Or pay someone $100-200/month to do it for you.

For a custom CRM or app: budget $200-500/month for ongoing maintenance and updates. This covers hosting costs, bug fixes, and minor changes as your business evolves. If someone builds you a custom app and says "it's done, you'll never need to touch it again," they're either lying or they don't know how software works.

This isn't unique to AI or custom software, by the way. Your truck needs oil changes. Your storefront needs a new roof every 20 years. Your website needs security updates. Software is the same. It's a living thing that needs care.

The businesses that get burned by custom software aren't the ones who build it. They're the ones who build it, don't maintain it, and then wonder why everything broke six months later. Don't be that business.


## The Bottom Line

Here's the decision tree, as simple as I can make it:

**If your business has fewer than 50 active customers and a simple workflow:** Use HubSpot free or Google Contacts + Sheets. Set up 1-2 Zapier automations. Total cost: $0-20/month.

**If your business has 50-500 customers and you're losing track of follow-ups:** Get a proper CRM (HubSpot paid tier or similar). Set up 5-10 automations connecting your tools. Total cost: $50-100/month.

**If you're paying for multiple software tools that don't connect, and you're wasting hours on manual data transfer:** Look into a custom solution. Budget $5,000-15,000 upfront and $200-500/month ongoing. But do the math first — make sure the savings justify the cost.

**If Excel still works:** Keep using Excel. I'm serious. The best system is the one that works, not the one that's newest.

The goal isn't to have the fanciest tech stack. The goal is to stop losing customers, stop wasting time, and stop paying for things you don't need. Sometimes that means building something custom. Sometimes it means a free tool and a little discipline.

You know your business better than any consultant, any software company, and any book author in Lancaster. Trust that knowledge. Use the tools that solve real problems. Ignore the ones that solve imaginary ones.
