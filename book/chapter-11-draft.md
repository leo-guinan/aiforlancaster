# Chapter 11: How to Hire an AI Consultant (Without Getting Burned)

Full disclosure: I'm an AI consultant. I live in Lancaster. I would love for you to hire me. And that's exactly why you should read this chapter carefully — including the parts that might talk you out of hiring anyone at all.

I wrestled with whether to include this chapter. It's like a barber writing a chapter on "Do You Really Need a Haircut?" There's an obvious conflict of interest. But I decided the conflict of interest is actually the point. If I can write honestly about hiring consultants — including the reasons not to — while being a consultant myself, that tells you more about how I operate than any sales pitch could.

So here's the deal. I'm going to give you everything you need to evaluate any AI consultant, including me. I'm going to tell you what fair pricing looks like, so nobody can overcharge you. I'm going to tell you the red flags, even the ones I've probably been guilty of at some point. And I'm going to tell you honestly whether you even need a consultant at all.

If you read this chapter and decide to hire someone else — or no one — that's a win. Because it means you made an informed decision, and that's all I ever wanted this book to do.


## The Five Questions to Ask Before Paying Anyone

Before you write a check, sit across the table from your potential consultant — or get on a Zoom call, I guess, since half of them don't leave their basement — and ask these five questions. The answers will tell you everything you need to know.

### Question 1: "Can you show me this working with a business like mine?"

Not a demo. Not a slideshow. Not a hypothetical case study with the client name conveniently anonymized.

A real business. Like yours. Ideally in your area, ideally in your industry or close to it. A business you could call and ask, "Did this actually work? Was it worth the money?"

If a consultant can't show you a working example with a business like yours, one of two things is true: they've never done this for a business like yours, or the businesses they've done it for weren't happy enough to serve as references. Neither of those is good.

I'm not saying they need to have worked with another Lancaster plumber if you're a Lancaster plumber. But they should have worked with a service business of similar size, with similar challenges, and you should be able to verify that.

### Question 2: "What happens when I stop paying you?"

This is the question that separates honest consultants from dependency machines.

When the engagement ends — and it should end at some point — what do you walk away with? Do you own the thing they built? Can it run without them? Is all the data yours? Can another developer pick up where they left off, or is everything locked behind proprietary code that only they can touch?

Or are you renting? Will the voice agent stop working if you cancel? Will the automations break? Will you lose access to your own customer data?

The answer you want: "You own everything. Here's the documentation. Here's how to run it without me. Here are the login credentials for every service. If you want to fire me tomorrow and hire someone else, you can, and nothing breaks."

The answer you don't want: "Well, it's all hosted on our platform, and we manage everything, and you don't really need to worry about the technical details." That's not a service. That's a leash.

### Question 3: "What does this cost you to run per month?"

This one makes consultants squirm, and that's how you know it's a good question.

If someone is setting up a voice agent for you and charging you $350/month for ongoing service, you have a right to know what the underlying costs are. The AI model might cost $30-80/month depending on call volume. Hosting might be $10-20. Phone number and telephony might be $20-50. So the hard costs might be $60-150/month, and the rest is their margin for maintenance, monitoring, and support.

That's fine. That's a reasonable margin. They're providing a service — monitoring, fixing issues, updating the system — and they should be compensated for it.

But if someone is charging you $1,500/month and the underlying costs are $75, that's a 95% margin, and they're banking on the fact that you don't know any better. Any honest consultant should be willing to have this conversation. Not necessarily show you an itemized receipt, but give you a straight answer about the general cost structure so you can see that the markup is fair.

If they won't answer this question, or if they get offended that you asked, walk away.

### Question 4: "What's your track record? Show me failures, not just wins."

Everyone has a highlight reel. I want to know about the blooper reel.

This is personal for me, so let me just put my cards on the table. I publish my track record. My accuracy rate is 42%. That means more often than not, my predictions about what will work don't pan out. I publish the misses alongside the wins because I think that's the only honest way to operate.

Now, 42% might sound terrible. "Why would I hire someone who's wrong 58% of the time?" Fair question. Here's the answer: the 42% that works generates enough value to more than cover the 58% that doesn't. And more importantly, I know my accuracy rate. I've measured it. I've published it. I can tell you exactly which types of projects tend to work and which ones are coin flips.

A consultant who tells you everything they touch turns to gold is lying. AI is new. The applications are new. Nobody has a 90% hit rate. If they claim to, they're either not measuring, not counting the failures, or not doing enough work to have a meaningful sample size.

What you want is someone who knows what they don't know and is honest about the uncertainty. Someone who says, "Here's what I think will work, here's why, and here's what we'll do if it doesn't."

### Question 5: "Will I own this, or do I rent it from you?"

This is important enough that I'm hitting it twice — once in Question 2 and again here — because it's the single most common way small businesses get burned by tech consultants of any kind, not just AI.

The answer should be: you own it. Period.

Your data. Your accounts. Your code. Your system. If they built you a custom app, you should have the source code. If they set up automations, you should have admin access to every account. If they trained an AI on your business data, that trained model should be yours.

"But Leo, if I own it all, what's to stop me from firing the consultant and doing it myself?"

Nothing. That's the point. The consultant should be confident enough in the value they provide that they don't need to hold your data hostage to keep you. If their only leverage is that you can't leave, they're not providing enough value for you to want to stay.


## Red Flags: When to Walk Away

Here's my list. I've seen every one of these in the wild. Some of them I've probably been guilty of in my early days, which is how I know they're red flags.

**They learned AI six months ago and now they're "experts."** The AI gold rush has created a lot of overnight experts. Someone watched a few YouTube videos, set up a ChatGPT account, and now they're charging businesses $5,000 to "implement AI solutions." Ask how long they've been building with AI — not using it, building with it. There's a difference between someone who's used ChatGPT to write emails and someone who's built custom AI systems. If their entire experience is from 2024 or later, be cautious.

**They use jargon to make you feel stupid.** "We'll implement a RAG-based architecture with fine-tuned embeddings on your proprietary data lake." If you hear something like that and your eyes glaze over, it's not because you're dumb. It's because they're trying to make simple things sound complicated so you feel like you need them. Any concept in AI can be explained in plain English. If they won't do that, it's because the plain English version would make you realize it's not as complex or as valuable as they're implying.

**They won't break down their costs.** I covered this above. Transparency about pricing isn't optional. It's a baseline requirement for trust.

**They promise specific revenue increases they can't substantiate.** "This AI system will increase your revenue by 30%." No, it won't. Or maybe it will. They don't know. Nobody knows until it's running. They can make projections based on assumptions — "If we capture 20% of the calls you're currently missing, and your average job is $500, that's X dollars per month" — and those projections can be reasonable. But a guarantee? A specific number? That's a sales tactic, not an analysis.

**They want a long-term contract before proving value.** A twelve-month commitment before they've delivered anything is a red flag the size of a barn. The honest version: a small paid pilot, maybe 30-60 days, after which you both evaluate whether it's working. If it's working, you continue. If it's not, you part ways. Anyone who needs a long contract to lock you in doesn't trust that their work will speak for itself.

**They don't have any local clients or references you can actually call.** This one matters more than people think. AI consultants are everywhere now — most of them remote, most of them in some city you've never visited. There's nothing inherently wrong with remote work. But if someone has zero clients in your area, zero references you can talk to face-to-face, and zero understanding of what a small business in Lancaster, Ohio actually looks like day to day, that's a gap. Not a dealbreaker, maybe. But a gap.


## Green Flags: Signs You Found a Good One

**They can demo live with your data.** Not a pre-recorded demo. Not screenshots. They take your actual business information — your menu, your services, your FAQ, whatever — and they show you the system working with it in real time. If it stumbles, they don't hide it. They show you the stumble and explain how they'd fix it.

**They explain everything in plain English.** You should leave every meeting understanding what they're going to build, how it works, what it costs, and why. If you feel confused after a meeting with a consultant, that's their failure, not yours.

**They tell you what AI can't do for your business.** This is the big one. An honest consultant should spend almost as much time talking about limitations as capabilities. "AI can handle your routine calls, but it's going to struggle with complex complaints — those should still go to a person." "This automation will work for your standard workflow, but the edge cases will need manual handling." If everything they say is positive, they're selling, not consulting.

**They offer a small paid pilot before a big commitment.** Notice I said "paid," not "free." A free pilot means they're desperate. A paid pilot means they value their time but they're confident enough to let you test the water before diving in. A pilot might be $500-1,500 for a limited implementation that runs for 30 days. At the end, you both look at the data and decide whether to continue.

**They have a physical presence or clear reputation in your area.** They show up to the Lancaster Chamber of Commerce meetings. They have other local clients. People in town know who they are. This isn't about being anti-remote. It's about accountability. Someone who lives in your town and does business in your town has a reputation to protect. That's a form of insurance you don't get with someone working from a laptop in a city a thousand miles away.


## What Fair Pricing Looks Like

I'm about to do something that might annoy other AI consultants: I'm going to tell you what things should cost.

**Voice agent setup:** $1,500 to $3,000. This covers building the agent, training it on your business information, testing it, and launching it. If someone is quoting you $10,000 for a voice agent setup, they're either building something far more complex than most businesses need, or they're overcharging. Ask why.

**Voice agent monthly service:** $200 to $350. This covers the AI costs, telephony, hosting, monitoring, and basic ongoing adjustments. The underlying hard costs are probably $60-150 of that, and the rest is their margin for support and maintenance. That's fair.

**Custom automation (Zapier/Make setup):** $2,500 to $10,000 depending on scope. A simple three-step automation connecting your website form to your CRM to your email? That's toward the low end. A complex ten-step workflow with error handling, conditional logic, and multiple integrations? That's toward the high end.

**Custom app:** $5,000 to $15,000. A simple custom CRM or workflow tool is on the low end. A multi-feature application with a customer-facing portal, integrations, and mobile access is on the high end.

**Monthly retainer for ongoing support:** $250 to $500. This is for the consultant to be available when things break, make minor adjustments, and keep things running smoothly.

Now. If someone is charging five times these numbers — $15,000 for a voice agent setup, $1,500/month for service, $75,000 for a custom app — they're banking on your ignorance. They're betting you don't know what things cost. Now you do.

Are there legitimate reasons for higher pricing? Sometimes. Complex enterprises, heavy customization, regulated industries with compliance requirements. But for a small business in Lancaster? These ranges should cover it.


## The Honest Truth About DIY

Here's where I talk myself out of some business.

Chapters 3 through 8 of this book cover what 80% of small businesses need. ChatGPT for daily tasks. A voice agent to answer phones. Social media assistance. Basic automation. If you followed those chapters — actually did the work, not just read them — you may not need a consultant at all.

Let me say that again, because it's important: you may not need a consultant at all.

The 80% path looks like this: you sign up for ChatGPT ($20/month), you set up a voice agent through one of the platforms I recommended (maybe $50-100/month), you use the free tier of a CRM, and you build a couple of Zapier automations. Total cost: under $200/month. Total time to set up: maybe 15-20 hours spread over a few weeks. No consultant required.

That leaves the 20%. The businesses that have genuinely complex needs. Who are they?

**High phone volume businesses.** If you're getting 50+ calls a day, the setup is more complex. You need proper call routing, overflow handling, integration with scheduling systems, and careful monitoring. This is hard to DIY well.

**Multi-location operations.** If you've got three locations with different hours, different services, and different staff, the complexity multiplies. The voice agent needs to know which location the caller wants. The CRM needs to track customers across locations. The automations need to route to the right people.

**Specific workflow problems that off-the-shelf tools don't solve.** This is the HVAC company with 30 crews, the property management company with 200 units, the medical office with compliance requirements. If your workflow is genuinely unique and no existing tool handles it well, that's when custom development makes sense — and custom development usually means hiring someone.

**Businesses where the owner's time is the bottleneck.** If you're a solo practitioner billing $300/hour, spending 20 hours setting up AI tools yourself costs you $6,000 in lost revenue. Paying a consultant $3,000 to do it in a week is the smarter math. This isn't about capability — you're probably smart enough to do it yourself. It's about the economics of your time.

If you don't fall into one of those categories, save your money. I mean that. I would rather you read this book and never call me than call me for something you could have done yourself in an afternoon. That's not false modesty. It's just math. If I help you with something you didn't need help with, I've taken your money for no good reason, and eventually you'll figure that out, and then I've lost something worth more than the fee: your trust.


## How to Structure the Engagement

If you've decided you do need a consultant, here's how to structure it so you're protected.

**Start with a paid assessment.** $500-1,000 for the consultant to evaluate your business, your current tools, your workflow, and your actual needs. At the end, they give you a written recommendation: here's what I'd suggest, here's what it costs, and here's the expected outcome. You can take that recommendation and implement it yourself, hire them, or hire someone else. The assessment should be valuable on its own, whether or not you continue.

**Do a pilot.** Whatever they recommend, start small. If they want to build a voice agent, do one phone line first, not all of them. If they want to build a custom app, start with the core feature, not the whole wishlist. Run it for 30-60 days. Measure the results against what they projected.

**Set clear milestones.** Not "we'll have it done in a few months." Specific dates, specific deliverables. "By week two, the voice agent is trained and running in test mode. By week four, it's live on one line. By week eight, we evaluate performance and decide on expansion." If they miss milestones, you know early.

**Get everything in writing.** What they're building. What it costs. What you own. What happens if it doesn't work. What the ongoing costs are. I know this sounds basic. You'd be amazed how many business owners skip this step with tech consultants because the technology feels complicated and they figure the consultant knows best. Get. It. In. Writing.

**Plan the exit from day one.** The engagement should have a natural end point. Not "we'll keep supporting you indefinitely." A specific plan for when and how the consultant walks away and your business keeps running.

Which brings me to the most important part of this chapter.

## Train Someone Inside Your Business

Here's what I think the best version of an AI consulting engagement looks like — and it's different from what most consultants will tell you, because it means they make less money.

Instead of building a system and then charging you $250/month forever to maintain it, the consultant should train someone who already works for you to run it. Modify it. Improve it. Own it.

Think about what that does for everyone involved.

**For you, the business owner:** You're not renting expertise. You're buying a transfer of capability. When the consultant leaves, the knowledge stays inside your building. You're not one canceled subscription away from your systems falling apart.

**For your employee:** The person you pick for this just learned the most in-demand skill in the economy. They became more valuable — not just to you, but to every employer. That's a raise waiting to happen. That's a reason to stay at your company instead of leaving for somewhere that offers more. You didn't just solve an AI problem. You created a retention tool.

**For the consultant:** This is the part most consultants won't tell you, because it sounds like bad business. It's not. Here's why. In a town like Lancaster — or any town where everyone knows everyone — the employee I train becomes my best referral source. They go to a Chamber event. Someone asks, "How did you guys get that voice agent working?" They say, "Leo set it up and trained me to run it." That's worth more than twelve months of maintenance fees.

Here's what this looks like in practice:

**The training engagement:** Instead of a monthly retainer that goes on forever, the engagement has three phases. Phase one: I build the system and get it running. Phase two: I work alongside your designated person, showing them everything — how it works, how to modify it, what to do when something breaks, and when to call me. Phase three: they run it solo for 30 days while I'm available for questions. After that, I'm done. They own it.

**Who to pick:** You want someone who's curious, not necessarily technical. The person on your team who's always the first to figure out the new software. The one who Googles things instead of asking. They don't need a computer science degree. They need to be willing to learn and not afraid of breaking things.

**The incentive alignment:** Here's the part I want you to really hear. If you train your office manager to run your AI systems and they get good at it, they're going to know they're worth more. And they are. The right move is to pay them more — because what they're doing is saving you money that used to go to the consultant, saving you time, and making the business more efficient. Pass some of that savings along. That's how you keep them.

This creates a cycle that benefits everyone: the business gets more capable, the employee gets more valuable and better compensated, and the consultant gets referrals from someone who actually understands what was built. Nobody is dependent on anyone. Everyone wins.

**What to ask a consultant:** "Is your plan to train someone on my team to maintain this, or is your plan to maintain it yourself?" If the answer is the latter, ask why. Sometimes there's a legitimate reason — maybe nobody on your team has bandwidth, or the system is genuinely complex. But most of the time, the real reason is that recurring revenue is more profitable than training engagements. And that's their incentive, not yours.

## The Final Word

The best AI consultant is one who makes themselves unnecessary as fast as possible.

I don't mean that as a platitude. I mean it as a business model. Come in, build the thing, train someone to run it, and leave. Collect a fair fee for your expertise. Get referrals from the people you trained. Move on to the next business.

If someone is building a dependency — if their business model requires you to keep paying them forever because nothing works without them — they're building their business, not improving yours.

The good ones come in, build something that works, put the knowledge in the hands of someone on your team, and walk out the door knowing the business is better off. Maybe you call them when you're ready for the next phase. Maybe you don't. Either way, the system runs without them. Your data belongs to you. Your employee knows how to maintain it. Your business is more capable than it was before.

That's the standard. Don't settle for less.

The best tool is the one that solves your problem. Sometimes that's a $20/month subscription. Sometimes it's a $15,000 custom app. And sometimes it's a book written by a guy in Lancaster who just wanted to make sure you didn't get ripped off.
