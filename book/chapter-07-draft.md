# Chapter 7: The $0 AI Audit — Walk Through Your Own Business

Print this chapter. Grab a pen. This is the most valuable exercise in the book.

I'm serious. If you're reading this on a screen, go print it. Or open a notebook. What we're about to do is worth more than any AI tool I could recommend, because we're going to figure out where *your* time actually goes before we spend a single dollar.

Most AI consultants want to sell you a solution. I want you to understand the problem first. Your problem. Not some generic "small business" problem — yours specifically.

This chapter is a walk-through. We're going to go hour by hour through a typical day at your business, and I'm going to ask you five questions at each stop. By the end, you'll have a clear picture of where AI might actually help you — and where it won't.

No software required. No account to sign up for. Just you, a pen, and some honesty about how you actually spend your time.


## The Hour-by-Hour Walk-Through

Pick a recent workday that felt normal. Not your worst day, not your best day. Just a Tuesday. Got one in mind? Good.

We're going to walk through it starting from when you first do anything work-related — and yes, checking email from bed at 6:15 AM counts.

For each hour of your day, I want you to write down what you were doing, then answer these five questions:

**Question 1: What are you doing right now?**

Write it down plainly. "Answering emails." "On the phone with a supplier." "Writing up an estimate." "Driving to a job site." "Staring at QuickBooks trying to figure out why the numbers don't match." Whatever it is, write it.

**Question 2: Is any of it repetitive text work?**

Emails where you're saying roughly the same thing you said last week. Forms you're filling out with the same kind of information. Product descriptions, job postings, social media posts, follow-up messages. If you've ever thought "I feel like I've typed this before," that's a yes.

**Question 3: Are you answering a question you've answered before?**

"What are your hours?" "Do you offer financing?" "How long does it take to get an appointment?" "What's included in the basic package?" If customers, employees, or vendors keep asking you the same things, write it down.

**Question 4: Are you playing phone tag or doing manual scheduling?**

You called them, they weren't there. They called back, you were with a customer. They left a voicemail, you forgot to return it until 8 PM. Or maybe you're going back and forth — "Does Thursday work?" "No, how about Friday?" "What time?" — through six text messages to book one appointment. Write it down.

**Question 5: Is information getting lost between you and someone else?**

A customer told your front desk one thing, but it didn't make it to you. You told an employee to order something, but it fell through the cracks. A lead came in from your website, but nobody followed up for three days. If stuff is slipping through gaps between people, that's worth noting.

Now do this for every hour. Yes, every hour. It'll take you maybe twenty minutes. I'll wait.

If you got more than ten, don't panic. That's normal. It doesn't mean you need ten AI tools. It means there's a lot of repetitive work in running a small business. You already knew that.


## The One-Page AI Opportunity Map

Now let's turn those Y's and M's into something useful. Take a fresh sheet of paper and make three columns:

```
TASK DESCRIPTION          | TIME SPENT/WEEK | COULD AI HELP?
--------------------------|-----------------|---------------
                          |                 | Yes / Maybe / No
                          |                 |
                          |                 |
                          |                 |
                          |                 |
                          |                 |
                          |                 |
                          |                 |
                          |                 |
                          |                 |
--------------------------|-----------------|---------------
TOTAL HOURS THAT COULD    |                 |
BE SAVED:        ____     |                 |
```

For each Y or M from your walk-through, write down the task, estimate how much time you spend on it per week, and make a judgment call: could AI actually help with this?

Be conservative with the "Yes" answers. If you're not sure, put "Maybe." If it involves physical work, complex judgment calls about people, or anything where getting it wrong would be a serious problem, lean toward "No" or "Maybe."

Add up the hours in the "Yes" and "Maybe" rows. That's your potential time savings. We'll refine this number in Chapter 8, but for now it gives you a ballpark.

Most business owners I've walked through this exercise in Lancaster end up somewhere between five and fifteen hours a week of potentially AI-assistable work. That's a half day to almost two full days. Not bad for twenty minutes with a pen and paper.


## What This Looks Like in Practice

Let me walk you through four examples from business types I see all the time around here. These aren't real people, but they're composites of real patterns. See if any of them sound familiar.

### The HVAC Contractor

Dave gets to his shop at 7:00 AM. First thing he does is check voicemails from last night — three people wanting quotes, one person with an emergency, two people asking if they can reschedule. He spends forty-five minutes returning calls. Reaches two of them. Leaves three voicemails. Phone tag has begun.

By 8:30, he's writing up two estimates based on yesterday's site visits. He pulls up an old estimate, changes the address and a few line items, and sends it out. He's done this roughly four hundred times.

9:00 to 3:00, he's on job sites. Good. This is the work. AI can't install a furnace.

At 3:30, he's back in the truck answering texts. "When can you come out?" "What does the warranty cover?" "Can I get a copy of that invoice from March?" He types the same answers he typed last week, with his thumbs, in a truck that's still running in a driveway on Cedar Hill.

By 5:00, he's doing paperwork. Entering job details. Writing up notes for the next day. Sending follow-up emails.

Dave's audit turns up about eight to ten hours a week of potentially AI-assistable work. The biggest chunks: phone tag and scheduling (three to four hours), writing estimates from templates (two hours), answering repetitive customer questions (one to two hours), and follow-up emails (one hour).

### The Restaurant Owner

Maria opens at 6:00 AM. Before the first customer sits down, she's already dealt with a call-off (now she's texting to find coverage), checked last night's numbers, and started working on this week's social media posts. She's staring at her phone trying to think of what to post about Wednesday's special.

The lunch rush is physical work. Cooking, managing the line, handling a complaint about a wrong order. No AI is going to help here.

But from 2:00 to 4:00, during the lull, Maria is answering the phone — "What are today's specials?" "Do you have gluten-free options?" "Can I book a table for twelve on Saturday?" — and she's also trying to update the menu on three different platforms (her website, Google, and DoorDash), writing a job posting because she lost another server, and responding to two Google reviews.

After close, she's doing inventory and placing orders.

Maria's audit: six to eight hours a week. Social media content (two to three hours), answering repetitive phone questions (one to two hours), responding to reviews (one hour), updating menus across platforms (one hour), writing job postings (occasional but painful).

### The Attorney

Tom gets to his office on Main Street at 8:00. By 8:15, he's reading emails — twenty-three of them. About half need substantive responses, but the other half are scheduling, status updates, and questions he's answered so many times he could recite them in his sleep. "How long does a closing typically take?" "What documents do I need to bring?" "What are your fees for a simple will?"

From 9:00 to noon, he's in meetings and doing actual legal work. Drafting documents, reviewing contracts, talking to clients. Some of this involves a lot of standard language that gets customized per client, but it requires his judgment. Maybe territory, maybe not.

Afternoon: more emails, returning calls, dictating notes from meetings that his paralegal will type up, trying to write a newsletter he promised himself he'd send monthly (it's been four months).

Tom's audit: five to seven hours a week. Email triage and drafting responses (two to three hours), answering standard client questions (one hour), drafting standard-language documents (one to two hours — this one's a "maybe" because accuracy matters enormously), and that newsletter he never writes (one hour per month, but the guilt takes up more space than that).

### The Retail Store Owner

Kim runs a shop on the square. She arrives at 9:00, opens at 10:00. That first hour is emails from suppliers, checking online orders from her Shopify site, and writing product descriptions for three new items she got in yesterday. Each product description takes her about fifteen minutes because she wants them to sound right.

During store hours, she's helping customers. That's the job. That's why people come to a shop on the square instead of ordering from Amazon.

But between customers, she's on her phone: posting to Instagram, answering DMs ("Do you have this in blue?" "What are your hours?" "Can you hold something for me?"), and trying to figure out what to do about the eleven items that have been sitting on the shelf for three months.

After close, she's reconciling the register, updating inventory, and writing thank-you emails to a few customers who made big purchases.

Kim's audit: seven to nine hours a week. Product descriptions (two to three hours), social media (two hours), answering repetitive DMs and messages (one to two hours), thank-you and follow-up emails (one hour), and writing promotional copy for sales and events (one hour, when it happens).


## What You Probably Noticed

Look at your own audit and look at these four examples. There are patterns. The same kinds of tasks keep showing up:

- **Repetitive writing.** Emails, descriptions, posts, estimates, responses. Different words, same structure, over and over.
- **Answering the same questions.** From customers, clients, patients, vendors. The same fifteen questions generate 80% of inquiries.
- **Scheduling and phone tag.** The most universally hated time-waster in small business. Everyone deals with it. Nobody likes it.
- **Information stuck in your head.** You know the answer, your employee doesn't. The customer told one person, but not the person who needs to know. Things fall through cracks not because anyone is careless, but because there's no system.

These patterns are exactly where AI is useful today. Not in some hypothetical future. Right now, with tools that exist, many of which are free or cheap.

They're also, and this matters, the kinds of tasks where AI making a mistake isn't catastrophic. If an AI drafts a slightly imperfect email and you fix two words before sending it, no harm done. If an AI answers "What are your hours?" incorrectly, that's annoying but fixable. The stakes are low enough that imperfection is tolerable.

That's the sweet spot. Repetitive, text-based, low-stakes, high-frequency. Write that on your opportunity map somewhere. Maybe circle it.


## What Didn't Show Up

Notice what's not on anyone's list: the actual work. Dave installing furnaces. Maria cooking. Tom giving legal advice. Kim helping a customer pick out a gift.

AI doesn't replace the thing that makes your business your business. It handles the stuff around the thing. The administrative wrapper. The operational tax you pay for the privilege of doing the work you actually care about.

If someone tells you AI is going to replace what you do, they're either wrong or they don't understand what you do. What it can do is give you back some of the hours that get eaten by the stuff around it.
