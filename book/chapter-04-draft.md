# Chapter 4: Email and Communication (Save 5 Hours a Week)

Let me guess how your morning goes. You sit down with coffee, open your inbox, and stare at forty-seven emails. Some need real answers. Some need polite answers. Some need carefully worded answers because the customer is upset and you don't want to make it worse. And every single one of them needs to sound like you — not like a form letter, not like a corporation, not like a robot.

So you start typing. You delete half of it. You rewrite the opening. You second-guess the tone. Forty minutes later, you've answered three emails and your coffee is cold.

I'm not going to tell you AI will write your emails for you. It won't. Not well, anyway. What it will do is eliminate the blank page. And if you've ever sat there with your cursor blinking, trying to figure out how to start a message to an angry customer or a flaky vendor, you already know: the blank page is where all the time goes.

## The First Draft Method

Here's the entire method, and it's not complicated:

1. You tell AI what you need to say.
2. AI writes a draft.
3. You read the draft, change what needs changing, and send it.

That's it. There's no magic. The reason it saves you five hours a week is simple math. Writing from scratch takes ten minutes per email on the hard ones. Editing a draft takes three. If you handle twenty difficult emails a week — and most business owners handle more — you just got back over two hours on email alone. Add in the newsletters, the job postings, the review responses, and the follow-ups, and five hours is conservative.

But here's the part that matters: the final email still sounds like you. Because you're the one editing it. AI gives you the clay. You shape it. Nobody on the receiving end knows or cares that you didn't start from scratch. They just get a clear, professional message that addresses their concern.

This is faster than writing from scratch AND the result sounds more like you than what AI wrote. You get the best of both worlds. The speed of a machine and the voice of a human who actually knows their customers by name.

## The Prompt Formula: Situation + Tone + Constraints

Every good email prompt has three parts. Memorize this structure and you can handle any message that lands in your inbox:

**Situation:** What happened? What's the context?
**Tone:** How should this sound? Friendly? Apologetic? Firm? Casual?
**Constraints:** How long? What must be included? What should be avoided?

Here's what that looks like in practice:

> "A customer emailed complaining about a late delivery. Write a response that is apologetic but honest, under 100 words, and offers a 10% discount on their next order."

That prompt takes fifteen seconds to type. The response you get back will be a solid draft — maybe 80% of the way there. You tweak a couple of words, add the customer's name, maybe mention the specific product, and hit send. Total time: two minutes instead of ten.

The more specific you are in each part of the formula, the better your draft will be. "Write a nice email" gives you garbage. "Write a warm but professional response to a longtime customer who's frustrated about a billing error, under 150 words, acknowledging the mistake and confirming the refund has been processed" gives you something you can actually use.

## Drafting Customer Responses

Customer emails fall into about a dozen categories. Once you realize that, you also realize you can have a prompt ready for each one. You're not reinventing the wheel every time someone asks for a refund or wants to know your hours.

Here's my approach: keep a document — a Google Doc, a note on your phone, a sticky note on your monitor, I don't care — with your most common prompt templates. When an email comes in, grab the right template, fill in the specifics, paste it into your AI tool, and edit the draft.

It's a recipe. Recipes work.

## Writing Job Descriptions That Actually Attract People

Most job descriptions read like they were written by a committee of lawyers who've never met an actual human. They're full of jargon, vague requirements, and phrases like "dynamic self-starter" that mean nothing to anyone.

AI can help you write job postings that sound like a real person at a real business offering a real job. Here's the prompt:

> "Write a job posting for a full-time customer service representative at a small hardware store in Lancaster, Ohio. The pay is $16-$18/hour depending on experience. No degree required. We want someone friendly, reliable, and willing to learn about home improvement products. The tone should be warm and straightforward — we're a small local business, not a corporation. Keep it under 300 words. Include that we offer health insurance after 90 days and are closed on Sundays."

Notice what happened there. You told it the job, the pay, the real requirements (not the inflated ones), the tone, and the details that actually matter to applicants. You'll get back something that sounds like a neighbor telling you about an opening, not a legal document.

The trick with job postings: be honest about what the job actually is. AI can polish your words, but it can't make a boring job sound exciting without lying. And lies get you applicants who quit in two weeks.

## Follow-Up Sequences: The 3-Email Follow-Up for a New Lead

Someone asks for a quote. You send it. Then... nothing. You know you should follow up, but you don't want to be pushy. So you don't. And the lead goes cold.

A three-email follow-up sequence solves this. Write it once, and you've got it forever. Here's the template:

**Email 1 (2 days after the quote):**

> "Write a brief, friendly follow-up email to a potential customer who received a quote from my [type of business] two days ago. Don't be pushy. Just check in, ask if they have questions, and remind them I'm happy to adjust the scope if needed. Under 75 words."

**Email 2 (5 days after the quote):**

> "Write a second follow-up email to a potential customer who received a quote from my [type of business] five days ago and hasn't responded to my first follow-up. Keep it casual and low-pressure. Mention one reason customers tend to choose us [insert your reason — fast turnaround, local reputation, warranty, whatever]. Under 75 words."

**Email 3 (10 days after the quote):**

> "Write a final follow-up email to a potential customer who received a quote from my [type of business] ten days ago. Be gracious. Let them know the quote is still good for 30 days, that there's no pressure, and that I'm here if anything changes. This is the last follow-up — make it feel like a door being left open, not a foot being jammed in it. Under 75 words."

Three emails. Spaced out. Each one gets shorter and more relaxed. I've talked to business owners who've closed 20% more quotes just by following up consistently. The hard part was never the selling — it was getting around to writing the emails. AI removes that excuse.

## Responding to Google Reviews

Google reviews matter. You know this. A good response to a positive review builds loyalty. A good response to a negative review can actually win people over — not the reviewer, usually, but the fifty other people who read the exchange before deciding whether to call you.

**For positive reviews:**

> "Write a short, genuine response to a positive Google review for my [type of business]. The reviewer's name is [name] and they specifically praised [what they mentioned]. Thank them sincerely without being over-the-top. Don't use exclamation marks more than once. Under 50 words."

The key here: don't sound like a corporate thank-you card. Sound like a person who's actually grateful. Change a word or two so it doesn't read identically to your other review responses.

**For negative reviews:**

> "Write a calm, professional response to a negative Google review for my [type of business]. The reviewer is upset about [specific issue]. Acknowledge their frustration without being defensive. Apologize for their experience. Invite them to contact me directly at [phone/email] to resolve it. Don't argue or make excuses. Under 75 words."

Two rules for negative review responses: never argue in public, and always take it offline. Your response isn't really for the upset reviewer. It's for every future customer who reads it and thinks, "Okay, this owner handles problems like an adult." That's worth its weight in gold.

## The Weekly Newsletter in 30 Minutes

A lot of small business owners know they should send a newsletter. Almost none of them do, because it feels like it takes forever. Here's the 30-minute method:

**Minutes 1-5: Outline.** Jot down three things worth mentioning this week. A new product, a seasonal tip, a local event you're involved in, a customer story (with permission), a behind-the-scenes update. Just bullet points. Don't overthink it.

**Minutes 5-15: Draft.** Give AI your outline and this prompt:

> "Write a short email newsletter for [business name], a [type of business] in Lancaster, Ohio. This week's topics are: [your three bullet points]. The tone should be conversational and friendly, like I'm talking to a neighbor. Keep the whole thing under 300 words. Include a brief sign-off that mentions [any call to action — visit us this weekend, book an appointment, check out the new arrivals, etc.]."

**Minutes 15-25: Edit.** This is the important part. Read every word. Change anything that doesn't sound like you. Add a personal detail — mention the weather, a local event, something that happened in the shop. Cut anything that feels like filler. Your customers can smell filler.

**Minutes 25-30: Send.** Paste it into Mailchimp or whatever you use. Hit send. Move on with your day.

Thirty minutes. Once a week. That's a newsletter that keeps you in people's inboxes and top of mind. Perfect is the enemy of sent. Send the thing.

## The 10 Copy-Paste Prompts

Here they are. The ten most common business emails, with prompts you can copy, paste, fill in the blanks, and use today. I'd suggest saving these somewhere you can grab them fast.

### 1. Customer Complaint Response

> "A customer named [name] emailed complaining about [specific issue]. Write a response that is apologetic and understanding, acknowledges the problem without making excuses, and offers [specific resolution — refund, discount, replacement, etc.]. Keep it under 100 words. Tone: warm but professional."

### 2. Quote/Estimate Follow-Up

> "Write a follow-up email to [name] who received a quote from my [type of business] for [service/product] [number] days ago. Check in to see if they have questions. Be friendly, not pushy. Mention that I'm happy to adjust the quote if their needs have changed. Under 75 words."

### 3. Appointment Reminder

> "Write a friendly appointment reminder email to [name] for their [type of appointment] at [business name] on [date] at [time]. Include the address: [address]. Mention our cancellation policy: [policy, e.g., 'please give us 24 hours notice if you need to reschedule']. Tone: friendly and helpful. Under 75 words."

### 4. New Customer Welcome

> "Write a welcome email for a new customer at [business name], a [type of business] in Lancaster, Ohio. Thank them for choosing us. Briefly mention one or two things that make us different [insert: e.g., family-owned for 20 years, free estimates, satisfaction guarantee]. Invite them to reach out if they ever need anything. Under 100 words. Tone: warm and genuine, not salesy."

### 5. Vendor Negotiation

> "Write a professional email to a vendor at [company name] requesting a better price on [product/service]. We've been ordering from them for [time period] and our volume has increased. I want to ask for a [specific request — percentage discount, free shipping, better payment terms] without being aggressive. Tone: respectful and businesslike. Under 150 words."

### 6. Job Posting

> "Write a job posting for a [position] at [business name], a [type of business] in Lancaster, Ohio. Pay: [range]. Hours: [schedule]. Requirements: [list real requirements, not wish-list items]. Benefits: [list what you actually offer]. The tone should be straightforward and welcoming — we're a small local business looking for a good person, not a perfect resume. Under 250 words."

### 7. Employee Policy Update

> "Write an email to my team of [number] employees announcing a change in our [specific policy — PTO, scheduling, dress code, etc.]. The change is: [describe the change]. The reason is: [brief honest reason]. It takes effect on [date]. Tone: clear and respectful. Acknowledge that change can be annoying but explain why this is happening. Under 150 words."

### 8. Holiday Hours Announcement

> "Write an email to our customer list announcing our holiday hours. [Business name] will be [open/closed] on [dates] and will return to normal hours on [date]. Tone: cheerful but brief. Wish them a happy [holiday]. Mention that they can [leave a message, email us, book online] while we're closed. Under 100 words."

### 9. Referral Request

> "Write an email to a satisfied customer named [name] asking if they'd be willing to refer us to friends or family. We recently [completed a project for them, served them, etc.] and they seemed happy with the result. Don't be awkward about it — just be honest that referrals are the best way our small business grows. If we offer a referral incentive, mention it: [incentive, e.g., $25 off their next visit]. Under 100 words. Tone: grateful and straightforward."

### 10. Thank You / Review Request

> "Write a thank-you email to a customer named [name] who recently [purchased something, used our service, completed a project with us]. Express genuine appreciation. Then ask if they'd be willing to leave us a Google review — include the link: [your Google review link]. Don't be pushy about the review. Make the thank-you the main point. Under 100 words."

There they are. Ten prompts. Print this section out if you want. Tape it to the wall next to your computer. The goal is that the next time you stare at an email and think, "Ugh, I have to figure out how to word this," you don't have to figure out anything. You grab the template, fill in the details, get a draft, and edit it.

## The Big Warning (Read This Part Twice)

I need to be direct with you about something, and I'm going to put it bluntly because it's important:

**Always read what AI writes before you send it.**

Always. Every time. No exceptions. I don't care if you're in a rush. I don't care if the first five drafts were perfect. Read number six too.

Here's why. AI will sometimes:

- **Get facts wrong.** It might say your store is open on Sunday when it isn't. It might reference a 10% discount you never offered. It might say "as we discussed on the phone" when you've never spoken to this person. These mistakes range from embarrassing to expensive.

- **Say something weird.** AI can produce sentences that are technically correct but sound off. Odd word choices. Phrasing that no human in Lancaster would ever use. A level of formality that doesn't match your business. You'll catch it instantly when you read it — but you have to read it.

- **Miss your voice entirely.** AI doesn't know that you always sign off with "Take care" instead of "Best regards." It doesn't know you call your customers by their first name. It doesn't know your sense of humor. These are the details that make your communication yours, and only you can add them.

- **Be too agreeable.** This is a subtle one. AI tends to over-apologize, over-promise, and say yes to everything. If a customer complaint isn't actually your fault, AI might write a groveling apology that implies liability you don't have. Read carefully. Edit accordingly.

The draft saves you time. The editing is non-negotiable. Think of it like a spell-checker. You wouldn't send an important email without glancing at the red squiggly lines. Same principle, bigger stakes.

I've heard a story about a business owner who let AI auto-respond to a customer complaint without reading the draft first. The AI offered a full refund on a $3,000 job for what was essentially a minor cosmetic issue. Could've been resolved with a thirty-minute callback. Instead it cost thousands because nobody read the draft. Don't be that person.

## Putting It All Together

Here's what your week looks like after you've got this system down:

Monday morning: open your inbox. Grab the right prompt template for each email that needs a real response. Generate drafts. Edit. Send. What used to take an hour takes twenty minutes.

Tuesday: a new Google review comes in. You grab the review response prompt, tweak the draft, and reply within the hour. The customer feels seen. Future customers see a responsive business.

Wednesday: you need to send the weekly newsletter. Thirty minutes, start to finish. Outline, draft, edit, send. Done before lunch.

Thursday: you realize you need to hire a part-time person for the summer. Instead of putting it off for two weeks because writing job descriptions is tedious, you grab the prompt, generate a posting, edit it, and have it up on Indeed by noon.

Friday: a lead from last week never got back to you on a quote. Follow-up email goes out in three minutes.

None of this is revolutionary. None of it requires expensive software or a computer science degree. It's just a system. Templates, drafts, editing, sending. The AI does the tedious part. You do the human part. And your week gets a little bit lighter.

That's five hours back. Minimum. Use them however you want — I'd suggest spending at least some of that time actually talking to customers face-to-face. That's the one thing AI will never be better at than you.
