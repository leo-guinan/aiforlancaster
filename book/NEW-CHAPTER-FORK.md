# Chapter 13: Fork This Book

I'm going to tell you something that would make any publisher's head explode: I want you to steal this book.

Not the way you'd steal a Stephen King novel — reading it without paying. I mean I want you to take the entire thing, change it, put your name on it, and publish it for your town. That's not piracy. That's the plan.

This book is licensed under CC BY-SA 4.0. In non-lawyer English: you can copy it, change it, share it, even sell it. The only rules are (1) give credit to the original and (2) license your version the same way, so the next person can do the same thing.

I'd rather 100 people copy this book for their own towns than sell 10,000 copies on Amazon. The goal was never the book. The goal was the network.

Let me explain.

## Why This Book Is Open Source

I live in Lancaster, Ohio. It's a good town. But it's not the only town with small business owners getting sold AI snake oil by people who drove in from Columbus or flew in from San Francisco.

Every small town in America has the same problem right now. The AI gold rush is real, and the consultants are coming, and most of them know less than they claim. I wrote twelve chapters explaining how to navigate that. Those twelve chapters work just as well in Zanesville as they do in Lancaster. They work in Waco, Texas. They work in Bangor, Maine.

But here's what doesn't work: me, personally, writing a version for every town. I know Lancaster. I know the businesses here, the chamber of commerce, the economic development people. I don't know Zanesville that well, and I've never been to Bangor.

You know your town. That's the whole thing.

If you live in a town and you understand AI — even at the level of "I've been using ChatGPT for six months and I know what it's good at and what it isn't" — you can create a version of this book for your community. You don't need to be an expert. You need to be honest. My track record is 42%. I'm not setting a high bar here.

## How to Create Your Own Version

This is simpler than it sounds.

**Step 1: Fork the repository.**

Go to github.com/leoguinan/ai-for-lancaster. Click "Fork." Now you have your own copy of the entire book — text, structure, everything.

If you don't know what GitHub is: it's where the files live. "Forking" just means making your own copy that you can edit. You'll need a free GitHub account. Takes two minutes.

**Step 2: Rename and localize.**

Find-and-replace "Lancaster" with your town name. Find-and-replace "Fairfield County" with your county. That gets you 80% of the way there.

The other 20% is the important part. Go through Chapter 12 — the local chapter — and rewrite it completely. This is where you talk about YOUR town:

- Which local businesses are already using AI well?
- What does the local business community look like?
- Are there co-working spaces, business groups, a chamber of commerce that's paying attention?
- What are the specific industries in your area? (Lancaster has glass manufacturing and logistics. Your town has something different.)

You don't need to rewrite Chapters 1 through 11 much. The five red flags of AI snake oil are the same everywhere. The free tools work the same in Oregon as they do in Ohio. The AI audit process doesn't change based on zip code.

But the local chapter? That's yours. Make it real. Use actual business names with their permission. Mention actual streets. The more specific it is, the more useful it is.

**Step 3: Set up a website.**

You'll want a local landing page. Something like aiforyourtown.com. It doesn't need to be complicated. A one-page site with:

- A way to download the book (PDF, free)
- An email signup for your local version of the prompt library
- Your contact information

This is where you build trust with your local community. The book is the proof you know what you're talking about. The website is how people find you.

**Step 4: Join the network.**

This is the part that makes it all work.

Once your version is live, submit a pull request to the main repository (or just email me at leo@metaspn.com) with your town name and website URL. I'll add you to the network directory. You add links back to other towns' versions.

That's it. Now you're part of something bigger.

## The Network Effect (In Plain English)

Here's the part that sounds too clever but is actually just basic math.

Google ranks websites based partly on how many other legitimate websites link to them. When "AI for Dubuque" links to "AI for Lancaster" and "AI for Lancaster" links to "AI for Dubuque," both sites get a small boost. Now add "AI for Bennington." And "AI for Waco." And "AI for Bangor."

Each town that joins the network makes every other town's site rank a little higher in Google. By the time there are 20 towns in the network, we all rank higher for "AI help for small business [town name]" than any individual site could on its own.

I've been calling this the SEO hivemind, which sounds more dramatic than it is. It's just websites linking to each other because they're genuinely related. Google rewards that. There's no trick. It's not a scheme. It's what Google is supposed to reward — a real network of real people providing real information to their local communities.

And it compounds. Every new town that joins makes the network more valuable for every town already in it. The first five towns benefit when the sixth joins. The first fifty benefit when the fifty-first joins.

I'm not going to promise you "10x results" because I'm the guy who leads with his 42% track record. But I can tell you the math works. Interconnected local sites with genuine local content rank better than isolated sites. That's not a prediction — it's how search engines have worked for twenty years.

If you create "AI for [Your Town]," you become the local AI person. Not because you gave yourself that title, but because you wrote the book — literally — on AI for your community. That's a different kind of credibility than a LinkedIn headline.

People will contact you. Business owners will ask for help. Some of them will be willing to pay for that help. Whether you want to build a consulting business, just help your neighbors, or something in between — that's your call.

I'm not going to tell you this is a get-rich-quick thing. It's not. It's a slow-build, high-trust, local-reputation thing. The same kind of thing that's worked in small towns forever — you help people, they tell other people, and eventually you've built something real.
