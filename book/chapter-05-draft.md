# Chapter 5: Marketing Without a Marketing Department

You know when you're supposed to be doing marketing? It's 9:47 PM on a Tuesday. The shop's been closed for three hours. You've eaten dinner, helped with homework, and now you're sitting on the couch with your phone, thinking you should probably post something on Facebook because you haven't posted in two weeks.

So you take a picture of... what? The front of your store? Again? You type something like "Come see us this weekend!" and stare at it for thirty seconds before deciding it's stupid and closing the app.

That's marketing for most small businesses in Lancaster. It's not a department. It's not even a job. It's a guilt-ridden afterthought squeezed between everything else you actually have to do.

I'm not going to tell you AI will fix this. It won't give you a marketing department. What it will do is make you roughly three times more effective in the time you're already spending — which, if we're honest, isn't much time. Three times almost-nothing is still not a lot, but it's enough to actually move the needle.

Let's get practical.

## Social Media Posts From Your Phone

Here's the method that actually works: batch your content. Instead of trying to think of something clever every few days (and failing), sit down once a week for one hour and generate five to seven posts at once. Schedule them. Forget about them. Move on with your life.

One hour. That's it. And with AI doing the heavy lifting on the writing, most of that hour is you deciding what to talk about, not how to say it.

### The Batch Content Method

Pick the same time every week. Sunday evening, Monday morning, whatever works. Then:

1. Jot down three to five things that happened in your business this week, or that are coming up next week. A new product came in. A customer said something nice. You're changing hours for a holiday. You learned something interesting about your industry. Just bullet points.

2. Feed those bullet points to AI with a prompt tailored to each platform.

3. Review what comes back. Edit it. Add a personal detail. Schedule it.

That's the whole system.

### Platform-Specific Prompts

Each platform has a different vibe, and your prompts should reflect that. Here are templates you can copy and modify:

**For Facebook (where your local customers actually are):**

> I own [type of business] in Lancaster, Ohio. Write a Facebook post about [topic]. Keep it conversational, like I'm talking to a neighbor. Two to three sentences max. Include a question at the end to encourage comments. Don't use hashtags. Don't use exclamation points in every sentence.

**For Instagram (when you have a photo to go with it):**

> Write an Instagram caption for a photo of [describe the photo]. I own [type of business] in Lancaster, Ohio. Keep it short — two to three sentences. Add 5 relevant hashtags at the end, including #LancasterOhio and #FairfieldCounty. Sound like a real person, not a brand.

**For Google Business Profile (this one's about search):**

> Write a Google Business update for my [type of business] in Lancaster, Ohio. Topic: [topic]. Keep it under 100 words. Include the phrase "[your business type] in Lancaster" naturally. Focus on what a customer searching for this service would want to know.

Notice what these prompts have in common: they all tell the AI to sound like a person, they set length limits, and they specify Lancaster. That last part matters more than you'd think. The AI's default mode is to write for everyone everywhere, and that's exactly what you don't want.

### Quick Graphics With Canva AI

If you set up Canva in Chapter 3, you already have access to their AI tools. For social media graphics, the Magic Design feature is the fastest path. Upload a photo of your product or shop, tell it what the post is about, and it'll generate a few layout options. Pick one, change the text if needed, download it, done.

You don't need to be a designer. You need to not look like you made it in Microsoft Paint in 2006. Canva clears that bar easily.

### The Authenticity Trap

Here's where I have to be honest with you, because this is where a lot of AI marketing advice goes wrong.

If you take the AI output and post it without changes, you will sound like every other business using AI to post without changes. And your customers will notice. Maybe not consciously — they won't think "this was written by ChatGPT." But they'll scroll past it, because it sounds like nothing. It's the verbal equivalent of beige.

Generic AI content hurts you more than posting nothing. Silence is neutral. Generic content actively tells your customers you don't care enough to be real.

The fix is simple: add one personal or local detail to every single post.

Not "We love serving our community!" That's generic. Instead: "The fog rolling off the Hocking River this morning made the whole shop smell like autumn. Good day for a hot coffee." That's specific. That's Lancaster. Nobody's scrolling past that.

When AI gives you a draft, your one job is to add the detail that makes it yours. Mention a street. Reference the weather. Talk about the guy who comes in every Thursday. Whatever is true and specific to your actual life in your actual business in your actual town.

The AI builds the frame. You hang the picture.

## Google Business Profile Optimization

Pull up your Google Business Profile right now. Seriously. Go to business.google.com and look at it.

If you're like most Lancaster businesses I've looked at, here's what I'll see: your name, your address, your hours (which might be wrong), and a business description that's either blank or one sentence long. Maybe a few photos from 2019.

This is free real estate. Google Business Profile is the single most important piece of online marketing for a local business, and most people treat it like a form they filled out once and forgot about.

### Your Business Description

You get 750 characters for your business description. That's not much — maybe four or five sentences. AI is perfect for this because you need to pack a lot of information into a tight space.

Here's your prompt:

> Write a Google Business Profile description for [business name], a [type of business] in Lancaster, Ohio. We [describe what you do in plain language]. Our customers are [describe your typical customer]. Include the phrase "[business type] in Lancaster, Ohio." Keep it under 750 characters. Sound professional but warm, not corporate.

Run it. Edit it. Make sure every fact is true. Post it.

### Services and FAQ

Google lets you list your services and add frequently asked questions. Most businesses leave these blank. Don't.

For services, just list what you offer. AI can help you describe each one:

> Write a one-sentence description for this service offered by a [business type] in Lancaster, Ohio: [service name]. Keep it clear and simple. Include what a customer should expect.

For FAQ, think about the five questions customers actually ask you on the phone. Then:

> I own a [business type] in Lancaster, Ohio. Write a brief answer (2-3 sentences) for this frequently asked question: [question]. Be helpful and specific. Include our location or service area if relevant.

### Responding to Reviews

We covered review response templates in Chapter 4, so I won't repeat all of that here. But the short version: respond to every review, positive or negative. AI can draft responses in seconds. A good prompt:

> Write a brief response to this [positive/negative] review for my [business type] in Lancaster: "[paste the review]." Thank them by name if they gave one. Keep it under three sentences. Be genuine, not salesy.

For negative reviews, always add the line about taking it offline — "Please call us at [number] so we can make this right." AI will usually include something like that, but double-check.

### Photos and Captions

Here's one thing AI cannot do: take pictures of your shop. No technology on earth can replace you pulling out your phone and snapping a photo of your storefront on a nice day, your team at work, your product on the shelf, or a customer's finished project (with their permission).

Photos are the number one factor in whether someone clicks on your Google listing. More than your description. More than your reviews. People want to see what they're walking into.

Take at least two new photos a month. That's it. Then let AI write the captions:

> Write a Google Business photo caption for a picture of [describe the photo] at [business name] in Lancaster, Ohio. One sentence. Natural tone.

Simple. But most businesses don't do it, which means doing it puts you ahead.

## Writing Your 'About Us' Page

Go look at your website's About page. If you have one, there's a decent chance it starts with "Founded in [year], [Business Name] has been proudly serving the Lancaster community..." and then says essentially nothing else.

Or maybe you don't have one at all.

Either way, we're going to fix it in about twenty minutes. Here's the method:

1. Open the voice memo app on your phone.
2. Talk for two minutes about your business. Why you started it. What you do. Who your customers are. What you care about. Don't think too hard. Just talk like you're explaining your business to someone at a cookout.
3. Transcribe it. You can use your phone's built-in transcription, or paste the audio into ChatGPT and ask for a transcript.
4. Feed the transcript to AI with this prompt:

> Here's a rough transcript of me talking about my business. Rewrite this as an 'About Us' page for my website. Keep my voice and personality — don't make it sound corporate. About 200-300 words. Include what we do, why we do it, and who we serve. Keep it warm and real.

Let me show you what this looks like with a real example.

### Before: The Voice Memo Transcript

"So yeah, we've been doing HVAC work for, I don't know, my dad started it in like '94 and I took it over when he retired. We do furnaces, air conditioning, all that. Mostly residential but we do some commercial stuff too, like small offices and things. Um, we're out in Lancaster but we go all over Fairfield County, sometimes out to Pickaway County too. I think what makes us different is we actually show up when we say we're going to, which sounds like a low bar but honestly in this industry that's not nothing. We've got four guys on the team now. Good guys. We don't try to upsell people on stuff they don't need, which — I've heard some stories from customers about other companies and it makes me mad, honestly."

### After: The AI-Polished About Page

"Mitchell Heating & Cooling has been keeping Lancaster homes comfortable since 1994, when Dave Mitchell started the business out of his garage on Cherry Street. His son Brian took over in 2018, but the approach hasn't changed: show up when you say you will, fix the problem, and don't sell people things they don't need.

We handle furnaces, air conditioning, heat pumps, and duct work for homes and small businesses across Fairfield County and into Pickaway County. Our four-person team has over 40 years of combined experience, and we still answer our own phone.

We know that when your furnace goes out in January, you don't need a sales pitch. You need someone at your door who knows what they're doing. That's what we do."

See what happened there? The AI took the rambling voice memo and made it clean, but it kept the personality. Brian's annoyance about upselling became "don't sell people things they don't need." The pride about showing up on time stayed in. It sounds like a real person runs this business, because a real person does.

One pass of editing. Twenty minutes total. And now you have an About page that actually makes someone want to call you.

## Monthly Newsletter in 30 Minutes

I know what you're thinking. "I can barely keep up with Facebook. Now you want me to send a newsletter?"

Yes. And here's why: you own your email list. Facebook can change their algorithm tomorrow and your posts stop reaching people. Instagram can decide your content violates some rule you've never heard of. Google can tweak their search results and your listing drops.

Your email list is yours. Nobody can take it away or throttle it. When you send an email, it lands in an inbox. Maybe they read it, maybe they don't. But it gets there.

And here's the thing — a monthly newsletter is not hard if you use a structure. Same structure every month. Four sections. AI writes the first draft of each one from bullet points you provide.

### The Structure

**Section 1: Business Update (what's new)**
What happened this month? New product? New team member? New hours? Expansion? Holiday schedule? Give AI three bullet points and let it write two paragraphs.

**Section 2: One Useful Tip (teach something)**
Share one thing your customers would find useful. A plumber might share a tip about preventing frozen pipes. A bakery might share how to store bread. A mechanic might explain what that dashboard light means. This is the section people will actually forward to friends.

**Section 3: One Offer (sell something)**
You're allowed to sell. One offer per newsletter. A discount, a new service, a seasonal special. Keep it simple. One thing, one call to action.

**Section 4: Personal Note (be human)**
Two to three sentences about something going on in your life or the community. Your kid's soccer season. The Fairfield County Fair coming up. That new restaurant downtown. This is the section that makes people remember you're a person.

### The Prompt

> I need to write my monthly newsletter for [business name], a [business type] in Lancaster, Ohio. Here are my notes for each section:
>
> Business update: [your bullet points]
> Useful tip: [your bullet points]
> Monthly offer: [your bullet points]
> Personal note: [your bullet points]
>
> Write a short, friendly newsletter with these four sections. Each section should be 2-3 sentences. Total length under 400 words. Use a conversational tone. Subject line should be short and specific, not clickbaity.

You'll get a draft back in about thirty seconds. Read through it, change anything that sounds off, add a detail or two that's genuinely yours, and hit send.

### Free Tools to Send It

You don't need to spend money on this:

- **Mailchimp Free Tier:** Up to 500 contacts, 1,000 sends per month. Plenty for most local businesses. Straightforward to set up. Has templates so you don't have to design anything.
- **Beehiiv Free Tier:** Up to 2,500 subscribers. Cleaner interface, more modern design. A little more setup but worth it if you plan to grow the list.

Either one works. Pick whichever one you find less annoying. The tool doesn't matter. Sending the email matters.

## When AI-Generated Content Hurts You

I've spent this whole chapter telling you how AI can help with marketing. Now I need to tell you when it does the opposite.

### Generic Posts That Could Be From Anywhere

"We're passionate about providing quality service to our valued customers! Stop by today and experience the difference!"

That could be a post from any business in any town on earth. It says absolutely nothing. It contains zero information. If you remove your business name from it and there's no way to tell who wrote it, it's garbage. Don't post it.

### Obvious AI Writing

AI has tells. Words and phrases it uses constantly that normal humans almost never use in casual conversation:

- "Delve into"
- "It's important to note that"
- "In today's fast-paced world"
- "Whether you're a... or a..."
- "Let's explore"
- "At the end of the day"
- "We pride ourselves on"
- Any sentence that starts with "Navigating"

If you see these in your AI-generated content, edit them out. Not because there's something wrong with AI writing per se, but because your customers can spot it, and it makes you look like you couldn't be bothered to write your own stuff. Which, fair, you couldn't. But they don't need to know that.

### Posting Too Much

There's a weird thing that happens when content becomes easy to create: people start creating too much of it. Suddenly you're posting every day because you can, not because you should.

For most local businesses, two to three posts per week on Facebook is plenty. One or two on Instagram. A Google Business update once or twice a month. A newsletter once a month.

More than that and you're just noise. Your customers follow you because they buy from you, not because they want a content feed. Respect that.

### The Rule

Here's the simplest test for any piece of content you're about to post:

**Would you say this to a customer standing in your shop?**

If a regular walked in and you said "We're passionate about leveraging quality solutions to enhance your experience," they'd look at you like you'd lost your mind. So don't post it either.

If you'd say "Hey, just got in a new shipment of that dark roast you liked — swing by this week before it's gone," then yeah, post that. It sounds like you. Because it is you.

## What This Actually Looks Like

Let me put this all together with a realistic scenario.

Say you own a hardware store on Main Street. Here's your weekly marketing routine:

**Sunday evening, 45 minutes:**
- Open AI on your phone
- Type in three things that happened this week: you got a new line of garden tools, a customer built a deck using lumber from your store, and you're closing early Wednesday for your daughter's graduation
- Generate five posts: two for Facebook, two for Instagram, one Google Business update
- Add a personal detail to each one — mention the customer's name (with permission), reference the weather, note that the garden tools are from an Ohio company
- Schedule them in Facebook and Instagram's built-in schedulers
- Post the Google Business update directly

**First of the month, 30 minutes:**
- Jot bullet points for your newsletter's four sections
- Run the newsletter prompt through AI
- Edit the draft, add your voice
- Send through Mailchimp

**Quarterly, 20 minutes:**
- Review your Google Business Profile
- Update photos, hours, services if anything changed
- Check that your description still reflects what you do

Total time: about an hour a week, plus a half-hour once a month. That's it. That's your entire marketing operation.

It's not a marketing department. But it's a heck of a lot better than posting a blurry photo of your storefront at 9:47 PM on a Tuesday and hoping for the best.
