# Chapter 9: AI Voice Agents — The Website of 2025

## The Analogy You Need to Hear

Cast your mind back to 2005.

If you were running a business in Lancaster back then, you probably remember the conversation. Someone — maybe a nephew, maybe a guy at Rotary, maybe that one friend who was always a little too excited about computers — told you that you needed a website.

And you probably said something like: "Why? Everyone around here knows where we are. We're in the phone book. We've got a sign out front."

A lot of business owners said exactly that. And for a while, they were right. The phone book still worked. The sign still worked. Word of mouth still worked.

But then something shifted. Slowly at first, then all at once. People stopped opening the Yellow Pages. They started typing things into Google. And the businesses that had websites — even ugly ones, even ones that were basically just their hours and a phone number on a white background — started showing up. The businesses that didn't have websites started disappearing. Not overnight. But steadily. Relentlessly.

The early adopters didn't have better websites. They just had websites. That was enough to create an advantage that lasted years.

I'm telling you this because voice agents are the website of 2025.

Right now, most small businesses don't have one. Most small business owners have barely heard the term. The ones who set one up in the next year or two are going to have an advantage — in customer experience, in efficiency, in revenue captured — that their competitors won't match for a long time.

That's not hype. That's pattern recognition. I've seen this movie before, and so have you.


## What a Voice Agent Actually Is

Let me strip away all the jargon.

A voice agent is software that answers your business phone. That's it at the core. But unlike the terrible automated phone trees you've been dealing with for the last twenty years — "Press 1 for sales, press 2 for support, press 0 to scream into the void" — a modern voice agent actually has a conversation with the person calling.

Here's what it can do:

**Understand what the caller wants.** Not from a menu of numbered options, but from natural speech. Someone calls and says, "Hey, I need to get my furnace looked at, it's making a weird noise," and the voice agent understands that this is a service request. Someone else calls and says, "What time do you close on Saturdays?" and it understands that's a simple question. Someone calls and says, "My basement is flooding and I need someone NOW," and it understands that's urgent.

**Book appointments directly on your calendar.** The voice agent connects to whatever calendar system you use — Google Calendar, your scheduling software, whatever — and can see your available slots in real time. It offers the caller a time, they agree, and the appointment shows up on your calendar. Done. No back-and-forth phone tag. No sticky notes that fall behind the desk.

**Answer common questions.** Hours, location, pricing, what services you offer, whether you're taking new clients, how to find the parking lot — all the questions you answer fifteen times a day and are tired of answering. You give the voice agent this information once, and it handles those calls forever.

**Take messages and route urgent calls.** When someone calls about something the voice agent can't handle — a complex problem, an angry customer, a genuine emergency — it takes a message, captures the caller's information, and sends it to you immediately. You can set rules: "If someone says the word 'emergency' or 'flooding' or 'gas smell,' call my cell phone right away."

**Work around the clock.** A voice agent answers the phone at 2 AM on a Sunday. It answers on Christmas. It answers when you're on vacation. It answers when you're in the middle of a job and your hands are covered in grease. It never calls in sick, never has a bad day, never puts someone on hold because it's dealing with another call. It handles multiple calls simultaneously, which is something even the best receptionist can't do.

That's it. No mystery. It's a tireless, reasonably smart phone answerer.


## What They Sound Like

If your mental model of an automated phone system is the robotic voice from 1998 that mispronounced your street name, I need you to update that image.

Modern voice agents sound remarkably human. Not perfect — we'll get to that — but remarkably close. They have natural cadence. They pause in the right places. They use filler words like "let me check on that" the way a real person would. They respond to what you're actually saying, not what a menu expects you to say.

You can have a back-and-forth conversation with them. You can interrupt them. You can change your mind mid-sentence — "Actually, wait, not Thursday, what about Friday?" — and they follow along.

They're not going to fool anyone into thinking they're human for an extended conversation. And they shouldn't. If a caller asks, "Am I talking to a real person?" a properly set up voice agent will tell the truth: "I'm an AI assistant for [your business name]. I can help you with scheduling, answer questions, or connect you with a team member." Honesty is the right approach here, and most callers genuinely don't care as long as their problem gets solved.

What callers care about is this: Did I get what I called for? If the answer is yes — the appointment is booked, the question is answered, the message is taken — they don't care whether it was a human or a machine on the other end. They care about the outcome.


## Who Benefits Most

Voice agents aren't for every business. If you're a solo consultant who gets three calls a week and enjoys talking to each one, you don't need this. If your business is mostly walk-in traffic, this isn't your priority.

But there's a specific type of business where voice agents are almost absurdly useful, and it's this: any business where the owner or employees are too busy doing the actual work to answer the phone consistently.

That covers a lot of ground in Lancaster.

**Service businesses.** HVAC companies, plumbers, electricians, cleaning services. These folks are out on jobs all day. Every call they miss is a potential customer who calls the next company on the list. A voice agent means that call gets answered, the appointment gets booked, and the customer never even knows you were elbow-deep in a water heater when they called.

**Medical and dental offices.** The front desk staff at a dentist's office spends an enormous amount of time on the phone — scheduling, rescheduling, confirming, answering the same questions about insurance and hours. A voice agent handles the routine calls, freeing up the human staff for the patients standing right in front of them.

**Hair salons and barber shops.** This is where the example gets really clear, and I'll dig into it in a moment.

**Auto repair shops.** "When will my car be ready?" "Do you work on Toyotas?" "How much for an oil change?" These are high-volume, repetitive calls. A voice agent eats these for breakfast.

**Any business where missing a phone call means losing a customer.** That's the common thread. If someone calls you, doesn't get an answer, and calls your competitor instead — you need a voice agent. Because that math is brutal. Every missed call is a missed chance at revenue, and you'll never even know it happened.


## The Barber Shop

This example comes from a podcast conversation that was one of the sparks for this book, and it's my favorite because it's so clean.

Think about a barber. Specifically, think about a barber in a one-chair or two-chair shop. The kind of shop you'd find on Main Street or over on Columbus Street here in Lancaster.

This barber is cutting hair all day. That's the job. Hands full. Clippers buzzing. Customer in the chair. When the phone rings, the barber has exactly three options:

One: Stop mid-haircut, put down the clippers, walk over to the phone, and have a conversation about scheduling while the customer in the chair sits there with half a haircut. This is bad for the customer in the chair, bad for the barber's pace, and bad for everyone's mood.

Two: Let it go to voicemail. The caller leaves a message — maybe. More likely, they hang up and call the next barber shop. Or they go online and book somewhere else. The barber loses the business and never knows it.

Three: Hire someone to answer the phone. For a one-chair barber shop, this is absurd. You're not going to pay someone $15-20 an hour to sit there and answer eight calls a day.

Now here's option four: a voice agent.

The phone rings. The voice agent picks up. "Hey, thanks for calling [barber shop name]. I can help you book an appointment or answer any questions. What can I do for you?"

The caller says, "I need a haircut this week, what do you have open?"

The voice agent checks the barber's calendar — the real calendar, in real time — and says, "I've got a slot Thursday at 2 PM or Friday at 10 AM. Either of those work?"

The caller picks one. The voice agent books it, sends a confirmation text to the caller, and adds it to the barber's calendar. The whole thing takes ninety seconds.

Meanwhile, the barber hasn't stopped cutting. The customer in the chair didn't get interrupted. The caller got their appointment. Everyone wins.

The pitch, in a sentence: Make more money. Never talk on the phone again.

That's not a gimmick. That's what it actually does. For a barber, or a plumber, or a cleaning service, or a mobile mechanic — anyone whose hands are busy doing the work that makes them money — a voice agent is the difference between capturing revenue and losing it.


## What It Actually Costs

Here's where I put real numbers on the table, because this is a no-hype book and you deserve to know what you're getting into.

A voice agent has a few cost components:

**The platform.** Voice agents run on platforms like Vapi, Retell, Bland, and a few others. These platforms charge by the minute of call time. Current rates are roughly $0.07 to $0.15 per minute, depending on the platform and features you need. This covers the AI processing, the voice synthesis, and the phone infrastructure.

**A phone number.** You need a dedicated phone number for the voice agent. This runs $1 to $2 per month. You can either forward your existing business number to it, or use the new number directly.

**Setup.** This is where the cost varies the most. If you hire a consultant — someone like me — to build and configure your voice agent, you're looking at $1,500 to $3,000 for initial setup. That includes designing the conversation flows, integrating with your calendar, loading your business information, testing extensively, and making sure it handles the weird edge cases your particular business encounters. If you're technical and want to do it yourself, the platform costs are minimal but the time investment is real. More on that in a minute.

**Monthly maintenance.** A voice agent isn't a set-it-and-forget-it tool. It needs monitoring and adjustment, especially in the first few months. A consultant will typically charge $200 to $350 per month for ongoing maintenance — reviewing call recordings, fixing issues, updating information, and improving the agent's responses based on real calls.

**Your actual per-call cost.** When you do the math, a typical 3-5 minute call costs you roughly $0.50 to $2.00 in platform fees. That's it. Fifty cents to two dollars to have a call answered, a question resolved, or an appointment booked.

Now compare that to the alternatives.

A part-time receptionist working 20 hours a week at $15-20 an hour costs you $1,200 to $1,600 per month, plus payroll taxes, plus the headache of managing another employee. And that receptionist only works during their scheduled hours. Calls at 7 PM? Voicemail. Calls on their day off? Voicemail.

A missed call that sends a customer to your competitor costs you whatever that job was worth. For an HVAC company, that might be $200 for a simple repair or $5,000 for a system replacement. For a barber, it's $25 for a haircut — but multiply that by a customer who might have come back every three weeks for years, and suddenly that missed call cost you thousands in lifetime value.

I'm not going to insult your intelligence by pretending the ROI math is complicated. It's not. If a voice agent catches even a few calls per month that would have otherwise been missed, it pays for itself. For most service businesses, the payback period is measured in weeks, not months.


## What Can Go Wrong

This is the section that a lot of voice agent vendors won't include in their pitch. I'm including it because I'd rather you go in with eyes open than get surprised.

**It will occasionally misunderstand people.** Heavy accents, strong background noise, people who mumble, people who talk in long rambling sentences without getting to the point — the voice agent will sometimes get confused. This isn't a hypothetical; it happens. The question isn't whether it will happen, but how often and how the system handles it. A well-built voice agent will recognize when it's confused and either ask for clarification or offer to take a message and have a human follow up.

**It can't handle truly complex or emotional conversations.** If someone calls crying because their pipe burst and their house is flooding and they're panicking, a voice agent can capture the emergency and route the call to you immediately — but it's not going to provide the emotional reassurance that a compassionate human can. If someone calls with a problem that requires real judgment, creative problem-solving, or empathy, the voice agent should hand that off quickly and gracefully. The good news is that most calls aren't that. Most calls are routine. The voice agent handles the routine and frees you up for the calls that actually need you.

**Some customers will hate it.** I'll be straight with you: there are people — often older customers, but not exclusively — who will not want to talk to an AI. Period. They want a human, and no amount of impressive technology will change their mind. You need a fallback plan for these folks. The simplest one: "If you'd prefer to speak with a person, I can take your name and number and have someone call you back within the hour." That usually works. The customer feels heard, you get their info, and you call them back when you can.

**It needs to be kept current.** Change your hours for the holidays? The voice agent needs to know. Add a new service? The voice agent needs to know. Raise your prices? The voice agent needs to know. If you don't update it, it will cheerfully give callers wrong information, which is worse than no information at all. This is the main reason ongoing maintenance matters. It's not that the technology breaks — it's that your business changes and the agent needs to change with it.

**The first month is the hardest.** No matter how well a voice agent is set up, the first month of real calls will surface issues nobody anticipated. Callers will ask questions you didn't think to prepare for. They'll phrase things in ways the agent doesn't expect. They'll try to do things the agent wasn't designed to handle. This is normal. It's also why you should plan to review call recordings regularly during that first month and make adjustments. By month two or three, most of the kinks are worked out and it runs much more smoothly.

None of these problems are dealbreakers. They're just the reality of working with a tool that's very good but not perfect. If someone tells you their voice agent never has issues, they're either lying or they're not listening to the call recordings.


## How to Evaluate a Voice Agent Vendor

If you decide to hire someone to set up a voice agent for your business — and for most people, that's the right move — here's how to separate the good vendors from the ones who are just riding the hype wave.

**The test call method.** This is the single best evaluation technique, and it's free. Ask the vendor for a demo line — a number you can call to talk to a voice agent they've built. Then call it. Not once. Five times. With different questions each time.

Call once and ask about hours. Call again and ask about pricing for a specific service. Call a third time and ask something weird — something a real customer might ask that's slightly off-script. "Do you guys do gift certificates?" "Can I bring my dog?" "What's parking like?" Call a fourth time and pretend to be confused or upset. Call a fifth time and ask to speak to a human.

Watch how the agent handles each scenario. Does it answer correctly? Does it handle the unexpected gracefully? Does it hand off to a human when it should? Does it maintain a natural conversation, or does it sound like it's reading a script? This tells you more about a vendor's quality than any slide deck or sales pitch ever will.

**Ask the right questions.** Beyond the test call, there are four questions every voice agent vendor should be able to answer clearly:

*"What happens when the AI doesn't know the answer?"* This is crucial. The right answer is some version of: it acknowledges it doesn't have that information, offers to take a message, and routes the question to a human. The wrong answer is: "That won't happen." It will absolutely happen.

*"Can I listen to recordings of all calls?"* The answer should be yes, without hesitation. You need to be able to review how the agent is performing with real callers. Any vendor who makes this difficult or charges extra for it is waving a red flag.

*"What's the process for updating information?"* Things change. Your hours change. Your pricing changes. You add or remove services. How hard is it to update the voice agent? Is there a portal you can use yourself, or do you have to email the vendor and wait? How long does it take for changes to go live? This is the nuts and bolts of ongoing maintenance, and you need to understand it before you sign anything.

*"What are your actual platform costs versus what you're charging me?"* This is a Leo Guinan special. I include it because transparency matters. If a vendor is paying $0.10 per minute in platform costs and charging you $0.50 per minute, that's a 5x markup. That's not inherently wrong — they're providing expertise, maintenance, and support — but you should know what the markup is. A vendor who gets squirmy about this question is a vendor who doesn't want you to understand the economics. You're a business owner. You should always understand the economics.


## The DIY Option

If you're technically inclined — or if you have someone in your life who is — you can build a voice agent yourself. I want to be honest about what that involves.

Platforms like Vapi and Retell are designed to be developer-friendly. They have documentation, tutorials, and APIs that a reasonably technical person can work with. There are also open-source options emerging that give you even more control, though they require more technical skill.

The realistic time investment for a DIY setup is 20 to 40 hours. That's not 20 hours of clicking buttons. That's 20 to 40 hours of learning the platform, designing conversation flows, integrating with your calendar and phone system, writing the prompts that tell the AI how to behave, testing extensively, and troubleshooting the things that don't work right the first time. Or the second time.

If you value your time at $50 an hour, the DIY route costs you $1,000 to $2,000 in time — comparable to hiring someone, but with the advantage that you understand your own system deeply and can modify it anytime.

The ongoing costs for a DIY setup are significantly lower: roughly $50 to $100 per month for platform fees and phone costs, depending on your call volume. That's compared to $200 to $350 per month when you include a consultant's maintenance fee.

The tradeoff is your time. When something breaks — and eventually something will — you're the one fixing it. When you need to update the agent's knowledge, you're the one doing it. When a caller reports a bad experience, you're the one reviewing the recording and figuring out what went wrong.

For some business owners, especially those who genuinely enjoy technology, this is the right path. For most, I'd recommend hiring someone for the initial setup and then deciding after a few months whether you want to take over the maintenance yourself. By then you'll understand how the system works and can make an informed choice.


## The Timing Question

I want to come back to the website analogy one more time, because the timing piece matters.

In 2005, the businesses that built websites didn't get better websites than the ones who waited until 2010. They just got them first. And those five years of being findable online while their competitors weren't — that compounded. They built up search rankings. They captured customers who then became loyal. They established an online presence that new competitors had to work hard to match.

Voice agents are at that same inflection point. The technology is good enough right now. Not perfect — it'll be better next year, and better still the year after that — but good enough to provide real value today. The businesses that deploy them now will capture the calls their competitors are missing. They'll provide a better customer experience than the competitors who are still sending everything to voicemail after 5 PM. They'll book appointments at 10 PM on a Tuesday when someone suddenly realizes they need to get their air conditioning serviced before summer.

And when their competitors eventually catch up in two or three years, the early adopters will have had two or three years of captured revenue and customer relationships that the latecomers can't get back.

I'm not saying you need to panic and get a voice agent this week. I am saying that if you own a service business and your phone goes unanswered even a few times a day, this should be at the top of your list.


## The Honest Bottom Line

Voice agents aren't magic. They're a tool. Like every tool in this book, they work best when a human is paying attention to how they're performing and making adjustments. You can't set one up and walk away forever. You can't expect it to handle every possible scenario with the grace of your best employee on their best day.

But you also can't answer the phone while you're under a sink. You can't be in two places at once. You can't work twenty-four hours a day, seven days a week, without burning out or making mistakes of your own.

For a service business in Lancaster — or Circleville, or Logan, or any of the small towns in this part of Ohio where people still call the phone number on the sign out front — a voice agent solves a real problem. Not a theoretical problem. Not a problem that only exists in Silicon Valley pitch decks. The real, daily, grinding problem of missing calls and losing customers because you were too busy doing the work to answer the phone.

The technology is here. The costs are reasonable. The ROI is clear.

For the right business, a voice agent is the single highest-ROI AI investment you can make right now. Not because it's flashy. Not because it's the future. Because it picks up the phone when you can't, and it turns missed calls into booked appointments.

That's not magic. That's just good business.
