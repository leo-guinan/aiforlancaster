# Chapter 8: Scoring Your Opportunities

You've got a list from Chapter 7. Maybe it's got five things on it, maybe fifteen. Either way, you're staring at it thinking: "Okay, now what?"

Now we prioritize. Because if you try to do everything at once, you'll do nothing. I've watched this happen. A business owner gets excited, signs up for four tools in one afternoon, configures none of them properly, gets frustrated by Thursday, and goes back to doing everything the old way by the following Monday. Don't be that person.

We're going to use a simple framework. Two axes, four boxes. You probably saw something like this in a management book once, or maybe on a napkin at a chamber of commerce meeting. It works because it's simple, and simple is what you need when you're running a business and don't have time to build spreadsheet models.


## The 2x2 Matrix

Draw a big plus sign on a piece of paper. You now have four boxes.

The horizontal axis — left to right — goes from **Easy to Implement** to **Hard to Implement**.

The vertical axis — bottom to top — goes from **Low Time Savings** to **High Time Savings**.

Label the four boxes:

```
                    HARD TO IMPLEMENT
                    |
    Quadrant 2:     |     Quadrant 4:
    PLAN FOR LATER  |     IGNORE
                    |
HIGH TIME __________|__________ LOW TIME
SAVINGS             |           SAVINGS
                    |
    Quadrant 1:     |     Quadrant 3:
    DO THIS FIRST   |     NICE TO HAVE
                    |
                    EASY TO IMPLEMENT
```

That's it. That's the whole framework.

**Quadrant 1: Easy to Implement + High Time Savings.** This is where the gold is. These are the things you can start doing this week with minimal setup that will give you noticeable time back. Do these first.

**Quadrant 2: Hard to Implement + High Time Savings.** These are real opportunities, but they require more setup, more money, or more technical help. Plan for these. Put them on the calendar for next month or next quarter. Don't ignore them, but don't start here.

**Quadrant 3: Easy to Implement + Low Time Savings.** Sure, why not. If you've got a slow afternoon and you've already tackled Quadrant 1, knock these out. They're easy wins, even if they're small wins. Nice to have.

**Quadrant 4: Hard to Implement + Low Time Savings.** Forget it. Seriously. These are traps. They'll eat your time, cost money, and save you twenty minutes a week. Every AI vendor in America wants to sell you something in Quadrant 4 and convince you it's in Quadrant 1. Now you know how to tell the difference.


## Scoring Your Items

Go back to your Opportunity Map from Chapter 7. For each item with a "Yes" or "Maybe," you need to answer two questions:

**How easy is this to implement?**

"Easy" means: you could do it yourself, today, with tools that already exist, for free or close to free. Signing up for ChatGPT and using it to draft emails is easy. Setting up an automated phone answering system with a custom AI voice agent is not easy — it's valuable, but it requires configuration, testing, and probably some help.

Here's my rule of thumb:

- **Easy:** You can do it with a tool you can sign up for in five minutes, and it works with copy-paste or simple typing. No integrations, no custom setup.
- **Medium:** You need to connect something to something else, or customize a template, or spend a few hours configuring. You could probably do it yourself, but it'll take a weekend.
- **Hard:** You need someone to build something, or you need to change a business process, or the tool requires real technical setup. This is "hire someone or dedicate serious time" territory.

For the matrix, "Easy" goes on the left side. "Medium" goes somewhere in the middle — use your judgment about whether it leans easy or hard. "Hard" goes on the right.

**How much time will this actually save?**

Be realistic. Not optimistic, not pessimistic. Realistic. Here are some honest estimates based on what I've seen working with businesses around Lancaster:

**Email drafting: 3-5 hours per week saved.**
This is the biggest easy win for most people. If you're writing more than ten emails a day — and most business owners are — having AI draft the first version saves real time. You still read it, you still edit it, you still hit send. But the "staring at a blank message trying to figure out how to phrase this" part goes away. For people who dread writing, this is life-changing. For people who are already fast writers, it's more like two hours saved. Still worth it.

**Social media content: 2-3 hours per week saved.**
If you're posting three to five times a week (which you probably should be, but probably aren't), AI can generate drafts of posts, suggest ideas, and help you repurpose one piece of content into several. You still need to add your voice, pick the photos, and make sure it doesn't sound like a robot wrote it. But the "I don't know what to post" paralysis goes away, and that alone is worth two hours of your week.

**Customer FAQ handling: 1-2 hours per week saved.**
This one's deceptive. An hour or two doesn't sound like much, but it's an hour or two of interruptions — the kind that pull you out of real work. If you can put your top fifteen FAQs somewhere that customers can find them (your website, an auto-response, a chatbot), you reduce the interruptions. The time savings are real but hard to measure because they're scattered across the day.

**Phone handling with a voice agent: 5-10 hours per week saved, but...**
This is the big one, and it comes with a big asterisk. An AI voice agent that answers your phone, handles basic questions, and books appointments can save you enormous amounts of time. Five to ten hours a week is realistic for businesses that get a lot of calls. But — and this is a significant but — setting one up properly is not a weekend project. It requires choosing a platform, scripting the conversations, testing it, handling edge cases, and making sure it doesn't make your customers feel like they're trapped in an automated phone tree from 2005. This is Quadrant 2 for most people. High savings, but hard to implement well. Plan for it, don't rush it.

**SOP creation: a one-time 10-20 hour investment, then it's done.**
Standard Operating Procedures — the "how we do things" documentation that every business needs and almost no business has. AI is shockingly good at helping you create these. You talk through how something is done, feed the transcript to an AI, and it spits out a structured document. You clean it up, and now you have an SOP. This isn't a weekly time savings; it's a one-time investment that pays off every time you train a new employee, every time someone asks "how do we handle this," and every time you need to remember your own process six months from now. Consider it a separate project rather than a weekly line item.


## The Monday Morning Test

Here's a quick gut check for whether something belongs in Quadrant 1.

Ask yourself: **Could I implement this before lunch on Monday?**

Not "could I theoretically start this on Monday." Could you actually have it working — as in, saving you time — by noon on Monday?

If yes, it's Quadrant 1.

If the answer is "well, I'd need to research the tools first," it's probably still Quadrant 1, but barely.

If the answer is "I'd need to set up an account, figure out the integrations, test it, and..." it's Quadrant 2.

If the answer is "I'd need to hire someone," it's definitely Quadrant 2.

The Monday Morning Test keeps you honest. It's the difference between doing something and planning to do something. Planning feels productive but it doesn't save you any time until the plan becomes action.

Let me give you some examples:

**Passes the Monday Morning Test:**
- Sign up for ChatGPT (or use the free version) and start drafting customer emails with it.
- Use AI to write this week's social media posts.
- Feed your employee handbook to an AI and ask it to create a FAQ document for new hires.
- Use AI to rewrite your "About Us" page that hasn't been updated since 2019.

**Does not pass the Monday Morning Test:**
- Set up an AI chatbot on your website.
- Implement an AI phone answering system.
- Build an automated workflow that takes a customer inquiry from your website, creates a record in your CRM, and sends a personalized follow-up email.
- Create a complete set of SOPs for your entire business.

Notice: the things that don't pass the test aren't bad ideas. Some of them are great ideas. They're just not Monday morning ideas. They're Thursday afternoon planning session ideas. They're next month ideas. They're "block off four hours and actually do this" ideas.


## Building Your Priority List

Take your Quadrant 1 items. You should have somewhere between two and five of them. If you have more than five, you're being too generous with what counts as "easy."

Now rank them by time savings. Biggest time savings first.

That's your priority list. Do them in order.

Here's what it might look like for those four Lancaster business owners from Chapter 7:

**Dave the HVAC contractor:**
1. Use AI to draft estimate emails and follow-ups (3 hours/week saved)
2. Create a standard FAQ response template for common customer questions (1 hour/week saved)
3. Use AI to write the "What to expect" email he sends before every job (30 minutes/week saved)

**Maria the restaurant owner:**
1. Use AI to batch-create social media posts for the week (2 hours/week saved)
2. Use AI to draft responses to Google reviews (1 hour/week saved)
3. Use AI to write job postings (occasional, but removes a painful task)

**Tom the attorney:**
1. Use AI to draft routine email responses (3 hours/week saved)
2. Use AI to create client-facing FAQ documents (1 hour/week saved, ongoing)
3. Use AI to draft that monthly newsletter he keeps not writing (1 hour/month, plus guilt reduction)

**Kim the retail store owner:**
1. Use AI to write product descriptions (2 hours/week saved)
2. Use AI to draft social media content (2 hours/week saved)
3. Use AI to write thank-you and follow-up emails (1 hour/week saved)

Notice something? Everyone's Quadrant 1 list involves writing. That's because writing is the task where AI delivers the most obvious, immediate value with the least setup. You type a prompt, you get a draft, you edit it, you're done. No integrations. No technical setup. No risk.

It's not glamorous. It's not "the future of business." It's practical time savings that starts this Monday.


## The Things That Didn't Make the List

Your Quadrant 2 items — the ones with high savings but harder implementation — deserve a page of their own. Write them down somewhere you'll actually see them. A sticky note on your monitor. A reminder in your phone for thirty days from now.

These are your next projects. After you've been using AI for the easy stuff for a month and it feels normal — because it will feel normal remarkably fast — come back to Quadrant 2 and pick the top item. Now that thing becomes your project.

The phone handling voice agent that could save Dave ten hours a week? That's a Quadrant 2 project. It's worth doing. It's just not worth doing first. Do the easy wins, build confidence, understand how AI actually works in your daily life, and then tackle the bigger stuff with realistic expectations.

Your Quadrant 4 items? Cross them out. Literally draw a line through them. They will tempt you. Some vendor will send you an email about an AI-powered solution that addresses exactly that item, and it'll sound amazing, and you'll think "maybe I should..." No. You shouldn't. The time savings don't justify the effort. That's what Quadrant 4 means. Move on.


## An Honest Note

You will not do all of this.

That's fine.

If you do ONE thing from your Quadrant 1 list, you're ahead of 90% of businesses your size. I mean that literally. Most small businesses are still in the "I should really look into that AI stuff" phase. They're thinking about it. Reading articles about it. Maybe they tried ChatGPT once to write a poem and thought "huh, that's neat" and then went back to typing emails from scratch.

If you take one task — just one — and start using AI to help with it this week, you've moved from thinking to doing. That's the gap that matters. Not the gap between using one AI tool and using ten. The gap between zero and one.

So pick one thing. The top item on your Quadrant 1 list. Start there.

It doesn't need to be perfect. Your first AI-drafted email is going to need editing. Your first AI-generated social media post might sound a little off. That's fine. Your first attempt at anything is a little off. You get better at prompting the same way you get better at anything — by doing it, noticing what works, and adjusting.

In Chapter 9, we'll get into the specific tools that are worth your time and money right now. But the tool doesn't matter if you don't know what to use it for. Now you know. You've got a list, you've scored it, and you've got a starting point.

Go do the thing.
