## Appendix A: The Prompt Library

These are copy-paste prompts. They work in ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or any other general-purpose AI tool. Customize the parts in [brackets] with your actual information.

I've organized them by what you're trying to do, not by what tool you're using. A good prompt works everywhere.

---

### Customer Communication

**1. Professional Response to a Positive Review**

> I own [type of business] in Lancaster, Ohio called [business name]. A customer left this positive review: "[paste review]". Write a warm, professional response that thanks them specifically for what they mentioned, feels genuine and not corporate, and is under 75 words.

**2. Professional Response to a Negative Review**

> I own [type of business] in Lancaster, Ohio called [business name]. A customer left this negative review: "[paste review]". Write a response that acknowledges their frustration without being defensive, offers to make it right, and invites them to contact me directly at [phone/email]. Keep it under 100 words and professional but human.

**3. Appointment Reminder Email**

> Write a friendly appointment reminder email for [type of business]. The appointment is [service type] on [date] at [time]. Include: what to bring or prepare, parking/location notes for [address], cancellation policy of [policy], and a warm but professional tone. Keep it under 150 words.

**4. Follow-Up After Service**

> Write a follow-up email to send 2 days after completing [type of service] for a customer. Thank them, ask if everything is working/looking good, remind them of any warranty or follow-up care, and gently ask for a Google review with this link: [link]. Keep it friendly, not pushy. Under 100 words.

**5. Estimate Follow-Up for Non-Responders**

> Write a follow-up email for a customer who received an estimate from my [type of business] 5 days ago but hasn't responded. Be helpful, not salesy. Acknowledge they may be comparing options. Offer to answer questions. Don't pressure them. Under 100 words.

**6. Seasonal Check-In Email**

> Write a seasonal check-in email from my [type of business] for [season — spring/summer/fall/winter]. Mention one relevant seasonal tip related to our services, remind them we're available if they need anything, and keep it genuinely helpful rather than a sales pitch. Under 125 words.

**7. New Customer Welcome Email**

> Write a welcome email for a new customer of my [type of business] called [business name]. Include: a thank you for choosing us, what to expect going forward, how to reach us [phone/email/hours], and one thing that makes us different from competitors: [differentiator]. Warm and professional. Under 150 words.

**8. Payment Reminder — Friendly First Notice**

> Write a friendly payment reminder for an invoice that's [number] days past due. The amount is [amount] for [service provided] on [date]. Keep it polite and assume it was an oversight. Include payment options: [list payment methods]. Under 75 words.

**9. Referral Thank-You Note**

> Write a short thank-you note for a customer who referred someone to my [type of business]. Mention the referral by first name: [name]. Express genuine gratitude. If we have a referral incentive of [incentive], mention it naturally. Under 75 words.

**10. Service Unavailability / Waitlist Communication**

> Write an email to a customer letting them know that [service/product] is currently unavailable and we expect it back by [timeframe]. Offer to put them on a waitlist. Suggest an alternative if applicable: [alternative]. Be apologetic but not groveling. Under 100 words.

### Marketing & Social Media

**11. Weekly Social Media Batch — Facebook/Instagram**

> I own [type of business] in Lancaster, Ohio. Generate 5 social media posts for this week. Include: one behind-the-scenes post, one customer-focused tip, one seasonal/timely post, one light/fun post that shows personality, and one that highlights a specific service or product: [service/product]. Keep each post under 50 words. Suggest a photo idea for each.

**12. Google Business Profile Weekly Post**

> Write a Google Business Profile post for my [type of business] called [business name] in Lancaster, Ohio. This week's focus: [topic — seasonal service, promotion, tip, event]. Include a call to action. Keep it under 100 words. Make it sound like a real person wrote it, not a marketing agency.

**13. Email Newsletter Draft**

> Write a short email newsletter for my [type of business]. This month's content: [1-2 topics]. Include a brief intro, the main content broken into scannable sections, and a simple call to action. Target length: 200-300 words. Tone: helpful and conversational, like writing to a neighbor.

**14. Local Event Tie-In Post**

> My [type of business] in Lancaster, Ohio wants to create a social media post tied to [local event — Fairfield County Fair, Lancaster Festival, Pilgrimage, etc.]. Write a post that connects our business to the event naturally without being forced. Under 50 words.

**15. Before/After Project Showcase**

> Write a short social media caption for a before/after photo of [project type] we completed. Mention the challenge, what we did, and the result. Keep it under 60 words. End with something that invites engagement without saying "like and share."

**16. FAQ Post Series**

> I get asked "[common question]" all the time at my [type of business]. Write a social media post that answers this question in plain language. Make it helpful enough that someone would save or share it. Under 75 words.

**17. Seasonal Promotion Announcement**

> Write a social media post announcing [promotion details] at my [type of business]. Include: what the offer is, when it expires, how to take advantage of it, and why we're running it (be honest — slow season, new service launch, customer appreciation, etc.). Under 75 words.

**18. Staff Spotlight Post**

> Write a social media post spotlighting [employee name] who works at my [type of business]. They've been with us [duration] and their role is [role]. One fun or interesting fact about them: [fact]. Make it warm and genuine. Under 60 words.

**19. Community Involvement Post**

> My business [business name] recently [sponsored/volunteered at/donated to] [community event or organization]. Write a social media post about it that focuses on the community, not on us. Mention what the event/organization does and why it matters. Under 75 words.

**20. Service Explainer Post**

> Many of my customers don't fully understand [service or process]. Write a social media post that explains it simply. Use an analogy if it helps. The goal is education, not selling. Under 75 words.

### Operations & Admin

**21. Standard Operating Procedure Draft**

> Write a standard operating procedure (SOP) for [task/process] at my [type of business]. Steps should be numbered, clear enough for a new employee to follow, and include any safety or quality checkpoints. Current process in my own words: "[describe your process roughly]". Format it professionally.

**22. Meeting Agenda Template**

> Create a meeting agenda for a [duration]-minute [type of meeting — staff, planning, review] at my [type of business]. Topics to cover: [list topics]. Include time allocations for each item and an "action items" section at the end.

**23. Customer Complaint Documentation**

> Help me document a customer complaint for our records. Here's what happened: "[describe situation]". Format this as a formal incident report with: date, customer info, nature of complaint, what we did to resolve it, outcome, and any follow-up needed.

**24. Vendor Comparison Summary**

> I'm comparing [number] vendors for [product/service]. Here are the details for each: [list what you know — price, terms, pros, cons]. Organize this into a clear comparison with a recommendation based on [your priorities — price, quality, reliability, local, etc.].

**25. Job Task Checklist**

> Create a daily task checklist for a [job title] at my [type of business]. They're responsible for [list general responsibilities]. Break this into opening tasks, mid-day tasks, and closing tasks. Make it specific enough to be useful, not so detailed it's patronizing.

**26. Email Template for Vendor Communication**

> Write a professional email to a vendor/supplier about [topic — late delivery, price increase question, new order, quality issue]. My situation: [describe briefly]. Keep it firm but fair. I want to maintain the relationship. Under 150 words.

**27. Policy Draft**

> Write a draft [type of policy — return policy, cancellation policy, employee PTO policy, etc.] for my [type of business]. Key details: [list the terms you want]. Make it clear and readable for [audience — customers, employees]. Avoid legal jargon but be specific enough to prevent misunderstandings.

**28. Training Outline for New Hires**

> Create a first-week training outline for a new [job title] at my [type of business]. They need to learn: [list key skills/tasks]. Break it into Day 1-5 with specific goals for each day. Include who they should shadow and what they should be able to do independently by Friday.

**29. Inventory Count and Order Template**

> Create a simple inventory tracking template for [type of inventory] at my [type of business]. Categories: [list categories]. Include columns for: item, current count, minimum threshold, order quantity, supplier, and last order date. Format as a table I can print or put in a spreadsheet.

**30. End-of-Year Business Review Prompts**

> I own a [type of business] in Lancaster, Ohio. Help me do an end-of-year review. Ask me 10 specific questions about how my business performed this year — revenue, customers, operations, marketing, employees, and goals. After I answer, help me identify the 3 most important things to focus on next year.

### Hiring & HR

**31. Job Posting Draft**

> Write a job posting for a [job title] at my [type of business] in Lancaster, Ohio. The role involves: [key responsibilities]. Requirements: [list them]. Pay range: [range]. What makes working here good: [honest selling points — not ping pong tables, real things]. Keep it straightforward and honest.

**32. Interview Questions for a Specific Role**

> Generate 10 interview questions for a [job title] position at my [type of business]. Mix behavioral questions ("tell me about a time...") with practical ones specific to the role. Skip the generic "what's your greatest weakness" stuff. I want questions that actually reveal whether someone can do this job.

**33. Employee Review Talking Points**

> I need to do a performance review for an employee in the role of [job title]. Their strengths: [list]. Areas for improvement: [list]. Help me structure talking points that are honest, specific, and constructive. I want to be fair and clear, not vague. Include 2-3 specific goals for the next review period.

**34. Rejection Email — After Interview**

> Write a brief, respectful rejection email for someone who interviewed for [position] at my business. They [mention something specific positive about their interview if applicable]. I want to be honest and kind without giving false hope or legal exposure. Under 75 words.

**35. Job Offer Email**

> Write a job offer email for [candidate name] for the position of [job title] at [business name]. Include: start date, pay rate of [amount], schedule of [hours], who they report to, and what to bring on day one. Be welcoming but clear on the details. Under 150 words.

Email 3: 60 days past due (final notice, mentions next steps). Keep each professional and under 100 words. My business is [type of business].

**A few notes:**

ChatGPT and Claude are where most people should start. They're general purpose, easy to use, and immediately useful. Try both on the free tier and see which one fits how you think.

Canva is the answer to "I need a social media graphic but I'm not a designer." It's not Photoshop. That's the point.

Zapier and Make.com are for when you're ready to connect tools together — like "when someone fills out my website form, add them to my email list and send them a welcome email automatically." You don't need this on day one. You might need it by month three.

Vapi and Retell are for businesses that live and die by the phone. If you're a contractor, a healthcare office, or any business where a missed call is a missed customer, these are worth investigating. They have a real learning curve, though. This might be where you want help.

## Appendix C: The BS Detector Checklist

Print this page. Take it to any meeting with an AI vendor, consultant, or that guy at the networking event who just started an AI company last month.

### Before the Meeting: 10 Questions That Flag Problems

Answer YES or NO. Each YES is a red flag.

**1. Are they guaranteeing specific results?**
"We guarantee a 40% increase in leads." Nobody can guarantee that. If they could, they'd be doing it for themselves, not selling it to you.
☐ YES (red flag) ☐ NO

**2. Is the pricing unclear or "custom" with no ballpark?**
"It depends on your needs" is fine for a first conversation. If you still can't get a number range by the second conversation, something's wrong.
☐ YES (red flag) ☐ NO

**3. Do they avoid answering "What happens if it doesn't work?"**
Any honest vendor has a plan for when things go sideways. If they won't talk about failure scenarios, they haven't thought about them.
☐ YES (red flag) ☐ NO

**4. Are they using jargon they can't explain simply?**
"We leverage neural network architectures to optimize your customer journey." Ask them to explain that in plain English. If they can't, they either don't understand it themselves or they're trying to intimidate you into buying.
☐ YES (red flag) ☐ NO

**5. Do they want a long-term contract before you've seen results?**
12-month minimums before you've seen anything work? No. A reasonable vendor lets you start small and expand. Lock-in contracts protect them, not you.
☐ YES (red flag) ☐ NO

**6. Are they dismissive of your current systems?**
"You need to replace everything." Usually you don't. Someone who respects your existing business will work with what you have, not demand you tear it down.
☐ YES (red flag) ☐ NO

**7. Can they show you a working example with a similar business?**
Not a case study from a Fortune 500 company. A real example with a business roughly your size, in a roughly similar industry. If they can't, you might be their first real client. That's not automatically bad, but you should know it.
☐ YES (red flag — they can't show one) ☐ NO

**8. Are they creating urgency that doesn't exist?**
"This price is only good until Friday." "Your competitors are already using this." Pressure tactics are a sign they're selling, not helping.
☐ YES (red flag) ☐ NO

**9. Will you own your data and content?**
If you stop working with them, do you keep everything they built? Your customer lists? Your content? Your workflows? If the answer is no or unclear, you're renting, not building.
☐ YES (red flag — you won't own it) ☐ NO

**10. Is their own business hard to verify?**
Google them. Check their website. Look for reviews. See how long they've been operating. If their LinkedIn was created last month and their website is a single landing page, proceed with extreme caution.
☐ YES (red flag) ☐ NO

### Scoring

Count your YES answers.

**0-3 red flags: Probably fine.**
No vendor is perfect. A couple of minor flags might just be clumsy salesmanship rather than malicious intent. Proceed, but keep your eyes open and start small.

**4-6 red flags: Proceed with caution.**
Something's not right. It might be fixable — maybe they're new and their sales process is rough. Or maybe they're not the right fit. Ask more questions. Get references. Don't sign anything today.

**7+ red flags: Walk away.**
This has "expensive lesson" written all over it. You can find someone better. The fact that you're reading this book means you're already smart enough to know that not every opportunity is a good one.

### The One Question That Matters Most

At the end of any meeting, ask this:

**"If I do nothing for six months and then come back, will this offer still be available?"**

If yes: good sign. They believe in what they're selling and they're willing to wait for you to be ready.

If no: ask why. There might be a legitimate reason (they're raising prices, limited capacity). But usually, the answer reveals whether they're focused on helping you or closing a deal.

### Final Rule

If your gut says something's off, it probably is. You've been running a business in this town long enough to know when someone's being straight with you and when they're not. AI doesn't change that. Trust your instincts. They got you this far.
